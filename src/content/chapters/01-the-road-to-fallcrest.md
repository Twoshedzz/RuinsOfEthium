The North Road wound its way through the great forests of northern Almenor like a pale ribbon cut through an endless sea of green. Towering oaks and pines crowded close to the track, their branches weaving together high overhead until only shafts of golden afternoon sunlight reached the road below. Moss covered ancient stones beside the path, birds called somewhere in the canopy, and a cool breeze carried the earthy smell of damp leaves and pine needles.

It was, Thorn Axehand decided, a very good place for an ambush.

He did not say so aloud. Instead he shifted the weight of his travelling pack and rested one hand on the haft of the axe strapped across his back. Although only a few days had passed since he had left home, travelling already felt strangely natural. There was always another bend in the road, another hill to climb, and another mystery waiting somewhere ahead.

Beside him padded Loki, his faithful guard dog. The big animal walked with his nose close to the ground, sniffing every interesting scent the forest had to offer. Every now and then he disappeared into the undergrowth for a few seconds before trotting happily back to Thorn's side again.

Behind them came Derek, steady as a walking barrel with boots. The dwarf carried himself with the calm patience of someone who expected trouble eventually, but saw no reason to hurry towards it. His shield hung across his back and his warhammer rested comfortably on one shoulder.

Dave followed next, clutching his spellbook as if it might run away. He was a copper dragonborn and still young by the standards of his people, but he stood six foot seven — exceptionally tall even for one of his kind, and tall enough that Thorn could always see his copper-scaled head above the ferns when they walked in single file. Every so often something small stirred beneath his cloak — a familiar resting in the pocket between worlds, Dave said, though he rarely bothered to explain further. He kept glancing at the trees, then at the road, then at the trees again. Every so often he muttered something under his breath and wiggled his fingers, practising the careful motions of a spell.

Nibbles McPicklepants brought up the rear, mostly because he had seen something shiny in the mud ten minutes earlier and was still hoping there might be more.

"You know," Dave said eventually, "I always imagined adventures would involve more dragons."

Thorn glanced over his shoulder. "We've only been travelling three days."

"I know, but dragons seem like the sort of thing that should happen quite early."

"They're probably saving themselves," Derek said.

Dave considered this. Before he could answer, a triumphant voice drifted from somewhere further behind.

"I've found treasure!"

Nobody stopped walking. Thorn sighed. "What is it this time, Nibbles?"

The dragonborn rogue emerged from the bushes holding something between two fingers. "A button."

There was a brief silence.

"It might have belonged to somebody important," Nibbles said.

"It might have belonged to somebody with a hole in their shirt," Derek replied.

Nibbles slipped the button into one of the many little pouches hanging from his belt anyway. "You never know."

That, Thorn thought, was true enough. The four companions had only known one another for a short time, but each was already becoming wonderfully predictable. Dave asked endless questions about magic, monsters and almost everything else. Derek quietly noticed things everyone else missed. Nibbles had never seen an object he did not want to investigate. And Loki simply assumed everyone they met would either become a friend or need barking at.

The road ahead dipped into a shallow hollow where an old fallen log lay half across the track. Moss covered its bark and weeds had grown around it, as though a storm had knocked it down years ago and the forest had begun to claim it. Scattered in the dust beside the log, catching the afternoon light, lay a small heap of glittering coins — far too conveniently placed to be anything but a lure.

Loki stopped walking. His ears stood upright and the cheerful wagging of his tail disappeared.

Thorn froze immediately and raised a hand for the others to halt. Derek noticed the change a heartbeat later. The forest fell silent — no birds, no insects, even the wind seemed to pause.

"Don't touch that," Thorn said quietly.

Nibbles had already taken two careful steps forward. He stopped, sighed, and crouched to study the coins from a safer distance. "It's not even good fake gold."

He picked up the nearest coin with the tip of his dagger, bent it between his fingers, and made a face. "Thin as cheese."

"So it's a trap," Dave whispered.

"Obviously," Derek murmured.

They spread out slightly and moved forward with more caution, weapons ready, eyes on the trees. Thorn kept his axe loose in his grip. Derek shifted his shield to the front. Dave clutched his spellbook with one hand and raised the other, ready to cast. Even Nibbles had the sense to stay low.

It was not cautious enough.

A twig snapped. Then another. Half a dozen small green figures burst from the undergrowth, waving rusty swords and crude spears. Their armour looked as though it had been stolen from a dozen different people and repaired with bits of string, while their pointed ears stuck out beneath battered leather helmets.

Goblins.

The largest of them scrambled onto the fallen log and spread his arms dramatically. "No fighting!" he squeaked. "We make bargain!"

Thorn did not lower his hand from his axe. "We've seen your bargain."

The goblin grinned, revealing a mouth full of unpleasantly sharp teeth — and the archers among them had already drawn their bows. Arrows hissed out from the trees. One thudded into the road beside Dave's foot. Another cracked against Derek's shield with a sharp ring. A third sailed past Nibbles' ear and buried itself in the fallen log with a dull thunk.

"Cheats!" Nibbles shouted.

The goblins howled with laughter. "Fake gold!" one cried. "Stupid travellers!"

Dave squeaked, raised both hands, and shouted the first spell that came into his head. A potato shot from his fingertips with surprising speed and struck the nearest goblin squarely between the eyes. The goblin on the log dropped backwards into the weeds.

Everyone stared. Even the other goblins stared.

Dave blinked. "That worked."

Thorn did not waste the moment. With a roar, he charged. Loki charged with him, barking furiously. Derek lifted his shield and moved after them. Nibbles tossed the bent coin aside, drew his blade and slipped between the trees to come at the goblins from the side.

The fight was short, loud and extremely confusing. Goblins darted from bush to bush, firing arrows, swinging blades and shouting insults that were not nearly as clever as they seemed to think. Thorn drove them back with heavy swings of his axe. Derek stood firm whenever they tried to rush the group, his shield ringing as blades glanced off it. Nibbles appeared from behind one goblin, jabbed it sharply, then vanished again before it could complain properly.

Dave fired another potato. This one hit a tree. The tree did not seem badly hurt.

Soon the goblins' laughter turned to panic. One threw down its sword and bolted into the forest. Another followed. A third, seeing Thorn and Loki bearing down on him together, made a noise like a kettle and fled north along a narrow track.

"They're running!" cried Nibbles.

"After them!" said Thorn.

"Is that sensible?" asked Derek.

"No," said Nibbles.

They chased them anyway.

The goblin trail led away from the road through brambles, over roots and up a muddy slope. At first they followed only the little footprints pressed into the wet earth, but Loki soon found a better path — a narrow animal track winding between the trees where something heavier than a fox had passed recently. Thorn crouched beside a splintered branch and noticed dark spots on the moss beneath it.

"Blood," Derek said quietly.

"Not much," Thorn replied. "But enough."

Whoever the goblins had dragged away had not gone quietly. The trail grew clearer as they climbed: torn cloth caught on a thorn bush, a discarded arrow, and once a battered wooden shield half-hidden beneath a clump of ferns. Derek brushed dirt from its painted surface and recognised the emblem at once — a soaring falcon above a stone tower.

"Fallcrest," he said. "This belonged to one of the town guard."

The cheerful mood faded. "Then the goblins have been busy," Thorn said.

Loki sniffed the shield, then the ground, then barked sharply towards the slope. Beyond the trees, half-hidden by ivy and grey stone, yawned the mouth of a cave. Smoke drifted lazily from somewhere within. The entrance faced south, cut into the hillside like a scowl.

The goblins had gone inside.

For a moment the companions stood at the foot of the slope and listened. From within came faint echoes: scurrying feet, goblin voices, and something that might have been crying.

Perched on a rough wooden platform above the entrance sat a single goblin guard with a shortbow across its knees. It did not look alert. It did not look fierce. It looked fed up.

The little creature yawned, scratched behind one pointed ear, and kicked a pebble down the slope with lazy contempt. Every now and then it glanced into the darkness behind it rather than out into the forest, as though whatever was inside the cave annoyed it far more than anything outside.

An angry voice echoed from underground. "Oi! Lookout!"

The guard sighed dramatically. "What?"

"Are you actually watching?"

"I *am* watching."

"You're useless!"

Another shout came from deeper in the hill. "Ratbag's nicked my dinner again!"

"Oh, for..." the lookout groaned. It stood up, slung its bow over one shoulder, and climbed down from the platform without giving the forest so much as a glance. "You lot sort it out yourselves," it grumbled. "I'm not going near Ratbag again."

The goblin disappeared into the cave.

The companions stared at the now unguarded entrance.

Before anyone stepped through, Dave murmured two careful words and held out one hand. A small shape unfolded from the air beside his wrist — a pseudodragon no larger than a house cat, with bright eyes and wings folded tight against coppery scales.

"Peggy," Dave said softly. Everyone called her Peggy. Dave called her Pegs, for reasons he had never explained and nobody had thought to ask about twice.

"Go look," he told her. "Quietly."

The little familiar slipped into the cave mouth and was gone.

She returned less than a minute later, landing on Dave's shoulder and chittering urgent nonsense into his ear. Dave listened, then nodded.

"Fighters," he said. "At least two goblin warriors deeper in — west side, she thinks. More voices to the east."

Thorn rested a hand on the handle of his axe. Derek adjusted his shield strap. Nibbles was already trying to decide whether treasure or prisoners should be searched for first. Dave looked at the dark opening and swallowed.

"I'll keep watch out here," he said. "Caves are dangerous. Someone sensible ought to stay where it's safe."

Nobody answered. Loki trotted confidently into the darkness. Thorn followed close behind. Derek went after them. Nibbles slipped inside with surprising speed, already scanning every shadow for hidden doors and stolen coin.

Dave remained where he was.

He looked at the cave. Then back at the forest. The trees seemed suddenly much closer than they had a moment before. A branch creaked somewhere high above. Somewhere in the undergrowth, something small cracked a twig.

"...Actually," Dave said to no one in particular, "keeping watch is also dangerous when there might be goblins behind you."

He hurried after the others. "Wait for me!"

His voice echoed down the tunnel as the daylight disappeared behind him.

The cave was cooler than the forest outside. Water dripped steadily from the ceiling, and the floor sloped gently downward through twisting passages lit by smoky torches wedged into cracks in the stone. Loki slowed to a cautious walk, his nose twitching as he sorted through dozens of unfamiliar scents.

The passage soon divided.

To the left, a tunnel bent west through narrower stone. To the right, a longer corridor wound east and north, the freshest goblin tracks pressed deep into the dust. Somewhere deeper underground came the unmistakable sound of someone crying — faint, but definitely human.

"We stay together," Derek said firmly.

Everyone nodded. Then they all looked at the tunnels again.

"We're never going to find whoever they're holding if we search one passage at a time," Thorn said quietly.

Derek did not like it. It showed on his face. "I know."

For several long seconds nobody spoke. Finally the dwarf sighed. "I still think this is a terrible idea."

"So do I," Thorn admitted. "But I think it's the only one."

They divided up.

Thorn, Derek and Loki took the eastern corridor with the freshest tracks. Wherever the boss and the prisoner were, that was the way. Dave and Nibbles went into the western tunnel, where angry voices echoed from somewhere ahead — voices far too interested in dice to sound like proper guard duty.

"Treasure never argues this loudly," Nibbles whispered.

"Neither do sensible people," Dave whispered back.

They crept forward together.

Thorn, Derek and Loki moved carefully along the eastern route. The tunnel narrowed, then opened into a stretch of floor that looked altogether too smooth. Derek stopped at once and held up a hand.

"Wait."

Loki halted, one paw still hovering above the stone. Thorn looked at the ground and saw it too — dust lying undisturbed in a perfect rectangle ahead of them, while goblin tracks veered sharply around it to one side. Someone had dug a pit here and covered it with branches and earth. It was not a deep drop, perhaps six feet, but deep enough to break an ankle or leave someone shouting for help while goblins laughed from the shadows.

"Trap," Derek murmured.

He led them the long way round, keeping to the solid stone at the edge of the passage. Loki followed without complaint, though he gave the hidden pit a deeply offended look as they passed. Thorn brought up the rear, axe ready in case any goblin had been waiting to see who fell in.

The great cavern opened before them not long after.

It was the largest chamber in the cave — wide, low-ceilinged, and cluttered with stolen goods. Crude torches jammed between cracks in the stone threw jumping light across broken crates, scattered packs and a rough wooden cage against the far wall. Inside the cage sat a young woman with dirt on her face, torn sleeves and a fierce expression that suggested she had been furious long before the goblins ever laid hands on her.

She looked up as Thorn stepped into the light, with Derek and Loki close behind him. "You're not goblins," she said.

"No," Thorn replied. "Are you hurt?"

"I'll live." She studied him for a moment. "My name is Mara. I was carrying a message to Fallcrest when those little monsters ambushed me on the road."

"Was the message important?" Thorn asked.

Before Mara could answer, a furious voice screeched from the shadows.

"Mine! Prisoner mine! Shiny bag mine!"

The goblin boss burst from behind a pile of crates, with two of his warriors scrambling out behind him. He was taller than the others, though only slightly, and wore a ragged red cloak that had clearly once been a curtain. In one hand he held a rusty sword. Padding at his side, snuffling and swaying on heavy haunches, came his pet — a giant rat the size of a large dog, with patchy grey fur, yellow teeth and a tail like a wet rope.

"My cave!" shrieked the boss. "My prisoner! My Ratbag!"

Ratbag squealed.

The two goblins fanned out to guard the cage, spears raised, eyes darting between Thorn, Derek and the dog.

Elsewhere in the cave, Dave and Nibbles had found the two goblin warriors.

They were crouched in a side passage with a pair of bone dice between them, shouting at each other with the kind of passion usually reserved for wars and weddings.

"You cheated!"

"I did not cheat! You just cannot count!"

"Five plus three is seven in *your* stupid head, maybe!"

Dave and Nibbles exchanged a look.

"I don't think we need to be quiet anymore," Nibbles said.

The goblins looked up. For a heartbeat all four of them simply stared at one another.

Then the warriors snatched up their blades and charged.

It was not an elegant fight. Dave hurled potatoes with more enthusiasm than accuracy. Nibbles darted in and out, stabbing at knees and elbows and any other target that presented itself. One goblin tripped over its own dice bag. The other took a potato to the chin and sat down very hard.

When the dust settled, both goblins were groaning on the floor and Nibbles was bleeding slightly from a shallow cut along one arm.

*The western tunnel — where an argument over bone dice became considerably less important.*

"Are you all right?" Dave asked.

"I've had worse from a bread knife," Nibbles said, though he did not sound entirely convinced.

They could hear the boss shrieking somewhere to the east. That seemed more urgent than bandages.

Back in the great cavern, the fight had already begun.

The goblin boss saw Loki at almost the same moment Loki saw Ratbag.

"No," said the boss.

Loki barked.

Ratbag panicked. The enormous rat wheeled around with a shriek and lumbered across the chamber, surprisingly fast for something the size of a large dog. Loki went after it without hesitation.

"Not Ratbag!" howled the goblin boss.

The chamber erupted. Thorn charged the boss with his axe raised. Derek threw himself at the two goblins guarding the cage, his shield up and his hammer ready. The boss ducked Thorn's first swing, slashed wildly with the rusty sword, and shrieked for his warriors to help.

For a few frantic moments Thorn had all he could do simply to keep the boss from reaching Mara. The goblin fought with the fury of someone who had just seen his favourite monster set loose in the same room as an enthusiastic dog. Thorn gave ground once, twice, then planted his boots and met the next blow on the haft of his axe.

Across the cavern, Derek fought with the steady, unshowy competence of someone who had done this before and preferred not to make a fuss about it. One goblin hurled a spear that rang off his shield. The other came at him with a rusty blade and was knocked flat before it could complain. The first goblin tried again, got caught by the hammer, and decided that loyalty to the boss had limits. Mara shouted encouragement from inside the cage, which helped rather more than anyone expected.

Meanwhile Ratbag had tried to squeeze beneath a broken table and failed halfway. Loki seized his chance. There was a furious scuffle, one enormous squeal, one triumphant bark, and the goblin boss turned just in time to see his beloved pet meet a heroic but unfortunate end.

*The great cavern — Thorn, Derek, Mara, Loki, and a goblin chief who had just lost an argument with a dog.*

"Ratbag!" he wailed.

That was the last mistake he made.

Thorn knocked the rusty sword from his hand and drove him to his knees with the flat of his axe. Derek smashed the cage lock with his hammer, and Mara stumbled free. The two goblins who had been helping the boss took one look at Thorn, one look at Loki — who was returning with the satisfied air of a dog who had just done something extremely worthwhile against an opponent twice his weight — and fled shrieking into the darker tunnels.

Loki trotted back to Thorn and sat, tail thumping once against the stone.

Thorn rested a hand on the dog's head. He did not say anything aloud. He did not need to.

He was, however, interrupted by a crash from the entrance passage, followed by Dave's voice shouting, "I can't see anything!" and Nibbles replying, "Stop standing on me!"

Derek closed his eyes briefly. "Tell me you did not."

"We were hurrying," Dave called up from somewhere below floor level.

Thorn crossed to the edge of the hidden pit — the second one, dug near the mouth of the great cavern where goblins had clearly intended to catch anyone rushing in to help. Dave and Nibbles looked up at him from the bottom, covered in dust and dignity in roughly equal measure. Dave's long legs were folded awkwardly beneath him. Nibbles, who had the misfortune of being underneath when they landed, looked deeply unimpressed.

"You missed the signs," Derek said, pointing to the goblin tracks that went around rather than across the suspicious patch of earth. "We nearly did the same thing on the way in."

"We were in a hurry," Nibbles said.

"So was the pit," Derek replied.

It took several minutes, a rope made from stolen sacking, and a great deal of muttering before everyone stood on solid ground again. By then the cave had fallen quiet except for Mara's breathing and the distant drip of water.

Derek checked the party's injuries with the calm efficiency of a cleric who had learned long ago not to ask too many questions about how people acquired them. He cleaned Nibbles' cut, pressed a cool hand to Dave's bruised shoulder, and made sure Thorn's arms were still attached in the correct order.

"Thank you," Mara said when he had finished with her. "All of you."

She retrieved a leather satchel from beneath a pile of stolen cloaks and clutched it tightly to her chest. Near the cage lay another piece of equipment — a round shield painted with the same falcon-and-tower emblem they had seen on the one by the road. Its surface was scorched and dented, but the magic woven into the wood still hummed faintly when Thorn's fingers brushed the rim.

"That belonged to my escort," Mara said quietly. "He didn't make it."

She looked at Thorn for a long moment, then held the shield out to him. "You're the one they couldn't knock down. Take it. I think he'd want it used by someone who actually needs it."

Thorn accepted it carefully. The shield was heavier than his own, balanced for a human soldier rather than a half-orc, but the enchantment settled into his grip as though it had been waiting. Once each day, Mara explained, it could flare with old protective magic — a brief ward that turned aside a blow meant to find flesh and bone. Not forever. Not often. But once, when it mattered most, it could be made into something stronger than ordinary wood and paint.

"That's what they wanted?" Thorn asked, nodding towards the satchel.

"I don't think they knew what it was," Mara said. "But someone did."

She opened the satchel just enough for them to glimpse the edge of old parchment inside. Strange marks covered it: lines, symbols and shapes that looked far older than any ordinary map.

"I was taking this to Fallcrest," she said. "To the wizard Nimozaran."

Derek's eyebrows rose. "The wizard?"

Mara nodded. "Or at least to his apprentice. It has to reach him."

"Then we'll get you there," Thorn said.

Mara studied him for a moment, then smiled. "I believe you."

Thorn searched the boss and the scattered crates while Nibbles prowled the rest of the chamber with the focused intensity of someone who believed every goblin in the world had stolen something personally from him. The goblin chief had not been a sophisticated thief, but he had collected a few worthwhile prizes. Thorn found a silver dagger tucked into the boss's belt — plain, well weighted, and faintly warm to the touch, with an edge that seemed sharper than ordinary steel ought to be. Nibbles counted out a handful of real coins from a locked box that gave up its secrets in under a minute, and Derek discovered a stoppered glass vial in a crate of stolen travelling gear: a potion of healing, still clear and faintly glowing.

"Real treasure," Nibbles said, considerably cheered. "Not buttons. Not fake gold. Actual money."

"And actual magic," Dave added, peering at the dagger over Thorn's shoulder. "That is definitely not normal."

"Neither is your cooking," Nibbles replied.

They agreed to divide the coin between them and let Derek keep the potion where he could reach it quickly. The silver dagger went to Nibbles, who had the best use for a blade that struck truer than common steel — though he spent the next five minutes admiring it anyway.

Leaving the cave took longer than entering it, mostly because Nibbles insisted on checking every remaining box, sack and crack in the wall in case the boss had hidden more. Most of what he found was useless. One sack contained turnips. Another contained socks. A third contained something that moved, so he wisely left it alone.

At last they emerged into daylight.

The forest seemed brighter than before. The danger had passed, at least for now. Behind them, the goblin cave lay quiet. Ahead, beyond the trees and the long curve of the North Road, waited Fallcrest.

Mara looked tired, but she walked with purpose at Thorn's side. Dave nursed his shoulder and complained that caves were statistically more dangerous than forests, which Nibbles said was only true if you counted treasure. Loki walked with his head high, still pleased with himself. Derek carried his hammer as though nothing remarkable had happened at all, which was how Derek preferred things.

"When we reach town," Mara said, "come to the Blue Moon Alehouse. I owe you a proper reward. Food, drink, coin if I can arrange it."

"Food?" said Dave.

"Drink?" said Derek.

"Coin?" said Nibbles.

"Loki likes sausages," said Thorn.

Mara laughed for the first time since they had found her. "I'll see what I can do."

So the companions returned to the North Road: Thorn Axehand with a new shield across his back, Derek the cleric, Dave the potato wizard, Nibbles McPicklepants and Loki, slayer of Ratbag.

They had left that road as wandering adventurers.

They returned as heroes of the North Road.

And far away, though none of them knew it yet, old magic stirred beneath ancient stone.
