They met Sergeant Murgaddin the next morning in the common room of the Nentir Inn, where Wisara had laid out bread, cheese and enough hot porridge to satisfy even Dave. The wreckage of the Blue Moon felt a long way away in the pale autumn sunlight streaming through the upper windows, though Thorn could still see a faint bruise on Nibbles' arm from the night before and Dave was guarding his new bottle of ink as though it might evaporate.

Murgaddin had brought a leather folder under one arm and a expression that suggested he had slept even less than they had.

"Sit," he said. "Eat if you haven't. Then listen."

He spread a rough sketch across the table — not a proper map, but enough to show a track leaving Fallcrest's northern edge, crossing farmland, and ending at a black mark beside a ruined building.

"The Hammers have been using an old manor house," he said. "Destroyed in the Bloodspear War, like half the places round here. The cellars survived. That's where they've made their nest."

Thorn studied the sketch. "How many?"

"Enough to be trouble. A handful of thugs, maybe a mage — we've had reports of lights down there after dark, and lights in a cellar usually mean someone who should know better." Murgaddin tapped the black mark. "I need someone to go in, break it up, and bring me proof of who's paying them. I've got guards, but not enough to spare for a proper raid, and the Lord Warden doesn't hand out soldiers to chase rumours."

"We're not soldiers," Thorn said.

"No." Murgaddin's moustache twitched. "But you're the ones who walked into a tavern full of Hammers and came out standing. That counts for something."

He opened the folder and produced a small cloth bag that clinked pleasantly, and a stoppered glass vial that caught the morning light with a faint inner glow.

"From the town," he said. "Coin, and a healing potion from the temple stores. Not a fortune, but Fallcrest pays its debts." He pushed them across the table. "There's more gratitude where that came from if you bring me something useful from that cellar."

Nibbles weighed the bag in his palm and looked considerably more enthusiastic about the morning.

Mara arrived as they were finishing breakfast, with Tobbs trotting at her heels and Loki at her side as though he had always been part of the delegation. Tobbs unrolled a larger map across the table — the road from Fallcrest towards the distant plateau, with a route marked in blue ink along the river and up towards the hills.

"For when you're ready," he said quietly to Thorn. "The manor first. Then Ethium. Nimozaran still needs those stones."

"We know," Thorn said.

"Do you?" Tobbs managed a tired smile. "Good. I'd hate to think I showed you a glowing rock for nothing."

---

The ruined manor stood less than an hour's walk from Fallcrest's northern gate, alone on a low rise above fields that had gone to thistle and long grass. Once it must have been handsome — Thorn could still see the bones of a grand entrance, tall windows now empty of glass, and the blackened timbers of a roof that had burned away decades ago. The orcs had done their work thoroughly on the upper floors. Only the stone shell remained, and whatever lay beneath it.

"Cheerful," Nibbles said.

"Profitable," Thorn replied, and led the way in.

The ground floor was a maze of fallen beams, ash and bird droppings. Loki's nose led them to a broken stairwell at the back of the house, where stone steps descended into cold air that smelled of earth, mildew and torch smoke. Thorn lit a torch from his pack. Derek raised his shield. Dave clutched his spellbook. Nibbles drew his silver dagger and looked pleased about it.

They went down.

The first cellar chamber might once have been a wine store or a servants' hall. Now it was a rough camp. Crates had been dragged in as tables and barricades. Muddy footprints crossed the floor in every direction. Three men wearing patched leather and iron hammer pendants on leather cords looked up as the torchlight spilled down the stairs.

One of them reached for a club.

He did not reach it in time.

Thorn was already moving. Loki bounded past him with a bark that bounced off the low stone ceiling. Derek planted himself in the doorway at the top of the steps and made sure nobody circled behind them. The fight was short and ugly — clubs against axe and hammer, Nibbles appearing where he was not expected, Dave flinching behind Derek until he found he could throw a potato without hitting any of his friends.

When it was over, three Hammers were groaning on the floor and the cellar was theirs.

"That went well," Dave said, sounding surprised.

"Don't sound so shocked," Nibbles told him, wiping his dagger on a crate. "We're professionals now."

A passage led deeper, where the air grew damper and a faint blue-green glow seeped from around a corner. Thorn raised his torch higher. The light fell on walls slick with moisture and clusters of mushrooms growing in the cracks — pale caps veined with soft luminescence, casting an eerie gentle light across the stone.

"Pretty," Dave said.

"Poisonous, probably," Derek said.

"Everything interesting is poisonous," Nibbles sighed.

Beyond the mushrooms, the passage opened into a larger chamber where a table had been set up with maps, coins, and a guttering lamp. Four men stood when they saw the torchlight — two Hammers with clubs and iron pendants, a swordsman in boiled leather with a long blade already half-drawn, and a broad figure in heavy plate who looked as though he had been hired precisely to stand between danger and whoever stood behind him. Behind them all, a thin man in dark robes rose from a stool with a curse and raised both hands. A crackle of sickly green energy spat towards Thorn and fizzed harmlessly against his shield.

The mage — Rulden, if the papers on his table were any guide — had not expected company.

He had even less time to complain about it.

Derek met the next bolt on his shield and went straight for the armoured man, hammer ringing against steel. Thorn closed with the swordsman, axe meeting blade in a shower of sparks. The two Hammers lunged together — and Nibbles slipped through the fungal gloom towards them, too quick for either to watch the half-orc with the axe and the dragonborn with the very sharp dagger at once. Dave, remembering the goblin cave, raised both hands and sent a potato sailing into the nearest Hammer's face with admirable accuracy.

Rulden snarled something under his breath and reached for a book on the table. Before he could finish whatever spell he had in mind, Thorn knocked the stool from under him and pinned his wrist to the stone floor.

"Don't," Thorn said.

The mage glared up at him, breathing hard. The fight had ended as quickly as it had begun.

They bound the survivors with rope from the camp and searched the chamber properly. Nibbles found a leather purse of gold coins and a scroll case tucked beneath the maps. Derek discovered a stoppered vial identical in make to the one Murgaddin had given them that morning — another healing potion, filched or bought, sitting beside a shortsword with a faint shimmer along the blade that suggested it was more than ordinary steel.

And on the table, half-hidden under Rulden's spellbook, lay an Ethium Stone.

It was smaller than the one Tobbs had shown them, but unmistakable — pale grey, blue-veined, humming with a dormant warmth that made the air around it feel slightly thicker. Dave stared at it with the expression he usually reserved for spellbooks and dragons.

"That's what they were guarding," Derek said quietly.

Rulden said nothing. His eyes flicked towards the stone, then away.

Dave, with Derek keeping watch, opened the mage's spellbook with careful fingers and began copying. The pages were cramped with notes, diagrams and spells in a hand that grew less steady towards the back. One spell caught his eye — a weaving of words and gestures meant to send enemies into unnatural sleep. He copied it twice, once into his book and once onto a scrap of parchment, mouthing the syllables as he wrote.

"I've got it," he said at last, looking up with soot on his cheek and triumph in his eyes. "Sleep."

"Useful," Thorn said.

"Very useful," Dave agreed.

They took the stone, the gold, the scroll, the potion and the sword. Nibbles wanted to take everything else as well, but Thorn drew the line at the table.

At the far end of the chamber, behind a stack of crates, they found a pair of heavy doors sealed with rusted iron bands.

"Family tomb," Derek said, reading the carving above the lintel. The name had been chipped away long ago. "This house had owners once. Proper ones."

"With excellent taste in doors," Nibbles said. "I wonder what's behind them."

"Gold," Nibbles added, in case anyone had missed his meaning.

Thorn tested the doors. They shuddered but held. Nibbles examined the hinges. Dave suggested a spell. In the end Derek and Thorn put their shoulders to the wood together while Loki barked encouragement, and the rust gave way with a shriek that echoed down the cellars like something waking.

Beyond lay a low stone room lined with four sarcophagi.

For a moment nothing moved.

Then the lids began to shift.

Skeletons sat up with the dry, horrible sound of bone on stone — four of them, yellowed and ancient, empty eyes turning towards the living with the mindless hunger of things that should have stayed buried. Thorn met the first with his axe. Derek's hammer cracked another apart. Nibbles danced back swearing. Dave, who had only just learned Sleep, tried it on the third skeleton with no effect whatsoever, which he felt was deeply unfair.

Loki did not care about fairness. He launched himself at the fourth skeleton's legs and brought it crashing down while Thorn finished the job.

When the last bones lay scattered across the floor, the tomb was quiet again.

Nibbles searched the sarcophagi with the focused energy of someone who felt owed compensation for the fright. He found more coins — old, tarnished, but real — and a few pieces of jewellery too damaged to wear but worth something to someone who knew how to melt gold.

"Family curse?" Dave asked, looking at the broken lids.

"Family problem," Derek said. "Let's not ask why."

---

They returned to Fallcrest in the late afternoon with scrapes on their knuckles, mushroom spores on their boots, and Rulden's correspondence tucked safely in Thorn's pack.

Murgaddin met them at Knight's Gate. He read the letter twice without speaking.

> **Rulden,**
>
> The arrangement stands. You will prove your worth now.
>
> A courier travels the north road within the next tenday, bearing a map recovered from the outer ruins of **Ethium**. It must not reach Fallcrest.
>
> Seize it. Kill the courier if needed, but bring the map intact.
>
> You are not the only ones searching. Others have begun to take interest in the old places. This cannot be allowed to spread.
>
> Deliver the map to my agent at the river crossing. You will be paid in full upon confirmation.
>
> Do not fail me again.
>
> — **Maelis Varn**, *Third Hand of the Valnaran family*

"They missed Mara," Thorn said. "We got there first."

"Aye." Murgaddin's voice was grim. "And they'd have tried again if you'd left them down there." He folded the letter carefully. "The Valnarans are one of the four guilds that make up the Gang of Four — trade racketeers out of Sunfall, the capital. They've allied with assassins, old thieves' brotherhoods and street brutes like the Hammers. Unstable arrangement, but it's held for years. And now someone's paying them to hunt Ethium."

"Someone in Sunfall," Derek said.

"Or further south." Murgaddin looked towards the distant plateau, invisible beyond the hills. "Their reach gets longer every season. You did well today. Better than well. Fallcrest owes you more than a potion and a bag of coin — but take these as well, for now."

He handed over another purse of gold, heavier than the first.

That evening they rested at the Nentir Inn. Dave copied the Sleep spell properly into his spellbook by lamplight, checking each line three times. Nibbles polished the magical shortsword and argued with Derek about whether identify scrolls were worth using on mushrooms. Thorn sat by the window with the Ethium Stone wrapped in cloth, turning it over in his hands without activating it. He could feel the faint warmth even through the fabric.

Tobbs came to see them after dark, with Mara and a satchel of road bread, dried meat and a fresh copy of the route north.

"Nimozaran is pleased," he said. "More than pleased. One stone is a beginning." He hesitated. "The manor was only a cell. There will be others. The map you saved Mara from carrying — they wanted that because of what's waking on the plateau. You have a stone now. You know the road. If you're still willing..."

Thorn looked at his companions.

Dave nodded so hard his horns nearly knocked the lamp.

Nibbles counted the new gold and said, "We're going."

Derek closed his prayer book. "Better to go now than wait for the next letter."

"Then you leave at first light," Tobbs said, relief plain in his face. "Follow the river road until it turns east. The blue mark on your copy shows where the old track climbs towards the hills — and the waterfall behind it, the only sensible way up. Make for the ruined tower first. If the stones are waking, that's where you'll feel it." He swallowed. "After that... you'll be deep in Ethium's shadow."

They slept, packed, and were at Wizard's Gate when the bells rang dawn.

The road east lay ahead — pale and empty, curving away between hedgerows towards the river and, beyond that, the distant plateau where old magic stirred beneath broken stone. Thorn Axehand led the way with his axe across his back and the new shield at his side. Derek walked steady as ever. Dave clutched his spellbook, now heavier by one spell. Nibbles had the silver dagger, the magical shortsword, and an identify scroll he intended to use on something impressive at the first opportunity. Loki trotted ahead, nose to the wind, utterly certain that whatever came next would be interesting.

Behind them, Fallcrest's walls grew small in the morning light.

Before them, Ethium waited.
