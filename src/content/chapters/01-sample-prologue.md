The road was still dark when Mara woke the others.

She had not slept well. Every time she closed her eyes she saw the same thing: a broken gate of black stone, half buried in ivy, standing alone on a hill where no hill should be. In the dream the gate hummed like a struck harp string, and something on the far side of it was listening.

"You're staring at nothing again," said Tobbs, rolling his blanket into a bundle with the practised speed of someone who had slept rough many times before. "If you're going to brood, at least brood quietly."

Finn snorted from beneath his cloak. "Leave her be. I dreamed of bacon."

"Dream harder," Mara said, though she smiled. She knelt by the cold fire ring and sifted ash through her fingers. The wind carried the smell of wet pine and distant rain. Somewhere far off a dog barked once and went silent.

They were three days from Fallcrest, the nearest town worth naming on any map they owned. Old maps, mostly — hand-copied, edges singed, ink faded to the colour of weak tea. One of them, Tobbs's favourite, showed a region labelled *Ethium* in careful script. The label had been scratched out so many times that the parchment was thin as a leaf.

"Ethium is a story," Finn had said when they first argued about the map. "A warning parents tell children so they'll stay on the path."

"Then why," Mara had answered, "do three separate merchants in two separate towns use the same word when they refuse to sell us rope?"

No one had a good reply to that.

![The black gate on the hill](/illustrations/prologue-gate.svg)

*The gate in Mara's dream — or memory. She could never quite decide.*

They broke camp before the sun cleared the treeline. The trail narrowed between roots and mossy boulders. Birdsong came and went in bursts, as if the forest itself were holding its breath.

By midday the rain found them — not a downpour, but the steady, patient kind that soaks through wool and temper alike. They walked with heads down until the path opened onto a ridge.

Below them the land rolled away in shades of green and grey. Fallcrest's smoke was a pale thread on the horizon. And beyond it, lost in haze, something darker: a line of hills that did not appear on Tobbs's scratched map at all.

Mara stopped walking.

"See it?" she said.

Finn shielded his eyes. "See what?"

"The gap in the world," she said. "Where the story begins."

Tobbs adjusted his pack and sighed the sigh of a man who had hoped for bacon and got prophecy instead. "Right," he said. "But first — Fallcrest. And if any of you mention Ethium in the tavern, I'm denying I know you."

They went on down the ridge, three small figures on a long road, while behind them the broken gate waited in Mara's thoughts — patient, humming, already open a crack.
