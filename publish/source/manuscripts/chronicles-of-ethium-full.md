# About This Book

This is the story of four young heroes who became caught up in the mystery of an ancient civilisation known only as **Ethium**.

The events in this book are based on our Dungeons & Dragons campaign. Every battle was fought, every difficult decision was made, and every ridiculous idea really happened around our gaming table.

Some conversations have been expanded and descriptions added so that the adventure reads like a fantasy novel, but the story remains faithful to the adventures of our heroes.

This manuscript is a living document. As the adventure continues, new chapters will be added and earlier chapters refined, until the campaign is complete.

# Dramatis Personae

## The Heroes

### Thorn Axehand
A brave young half-orc fighter who rarely hesitates when danger appears. Armed with his trusty axe and an even stronger sense of loyalty, Thorn often finds himself leading the group into adventure.

### Loki
Thorn's faithful guard dog. Fearless, loyal and blessed with an excellent nose, Loki has an uncanny habit of finding trouble—and occasionally saving the day.

### Derek
A dependable dwarven cleric named after one of the greatest heroes from *Stranger Things*. Calm under pressure and always ready to protect his friends, Derek is the heart of the group.

### Dave
A very young copper dragonborn wizard whose favourite spell launches magically propelled potatoes instead of glowing bolts of force. Despite his unusual style, his magic has saved the party more than once. When truly threatened, he can unleash the poisonous breath of his dragon ancestors.

### Nibbles McPicklepants
A dragonborn rogue with quick hands, sharp eyes and an endless curiosity. Nibbles is always searching for hidden treasure, secret passages or his next upgrade. One day he hopes to own enough poison to coat every blade he carries.

# Prologue

The North Road had always been a dangerous place.

Travellers spoke of wolves in the forests, bandits on lonely stretches of road and stranger things lurking amongst forgotten ruins. Yet during recent months even seasoned merchants had begun whispering about goblins growing bolder than ever before. Caravans vanished. Couriers disappeared. Something was changing in the wild lands beyond civilisation.

Most people chose safer roads.

Four young adventurers chose the North Road instead.

There was Thorn Axehand, a fearless half-orc who believed courage could solve almost any problem. Walking faithfully beside him padded Loki, a loyal guard dog with an excellent nose and absolutely no fear.

Travelling alongside them came Derek, a sturdy dwarven cleric whose calm judgement often prevented the others from getting themselves into even greater trouble.

Dave, the youngest member of the party, was a copper dragonborn wizard with an unusual talent. While other wizards studied elegant spells of force and fire, Dave somehow managed to transform one of the most famous spells in the world into a barrage of flying potatoes. Nobody—not even Dave himself—could quite explain why.

Finally there was Nibbles McPicklepants, a dragonborn rogue whose curiosity was almost as dangerous as the monsters they would soon encounter. If there was treasure to be found, Nibbles would usually discover it first.

None of them knew each other particularly well.

None of them had any idea what awaited them.

And none of them had ever heard the name...

**Ethium.**

# Chapter One
# The Road to Fallcrest
The North Road ran like a pale scar through the forest.
On either side, dark trees crowded close, their branches knitting together overhead. The afternoon sun slipped through in thin golden shafts, lighting patches of moss, fern and old stone. Somewhere far away, a bird called once, then fell silent.
Thorn Axehand walked near the front.
He liked the front. The front was where danger usually appeared first, and Thorn preferred danger where he could see it. His axe rested across one broad shoulder, and beside him padded Loki, nose low to the ground, tail swishing whenever he caught an interesting scent.
Behind them came Derek, steady as a walking barrel with boots. The dwarf carried himself with the calm patience of someone who expected trouble eventually, but saw no reason to hurry towards it.
Dave followed next, clutching his spellbook as if it might run away. The young copper dragonborn kept glancing at the trees, then at the road, then at the trees again. Every so often he muttered something under his breath and wiggled his fingers, practising the careful motions of a spell.
Nibbles McPicklepants brought up the rear.
Not because he had been asked to.
Not because he was keeping watch.
Mostly because he had seen something shiny in the mud ten minutes earlier and was still hoping there might be more.
"Are we nearly there?" Dave asked.
"Nearly where?" said Nibbles.
"Fallcrest."
"Probably."
"Is probably good?"
"Probably."
Thorn glanced back. "Keep your eyes open."
Nibbles immediately opened his eyes as wide as possible.
"I mean watch the trees," Thorn said.
"Oh."
The road ahead dipped into a shallow hollow where an old fallen log lay half across the track. It looked as though a storm had knocked it down years ago. Moss covered its bark, and weeds had grown around it.
Loki stopped.
His ears lifted.
Thorn stopped too.
Derek noticed at once. "What is it?"
Loki growled.
The forest went quiet.
A twig snapped.
Then the goblins attacked.
They came shrieking from the undergrowth, small green shapes with sharp teeth, sharper blades and far too much confidence. One leapt onto the fallen log and waved a rusty sword above its head.
"Gold!" it shouted in a cracked little voice. "Gold for you!"
It flung a handful of glittering coins into the road.
For one glorious second, Nibbles McPicklepants forgot everything else in the world.
"Treasure!"
He darted forward.
"Nibbles, wait!" Thorn yelled.
Too late.
The coins bounced and spun across the dirt. They flashed beautifully in the sunlight. They looked perfect.
They were not perfect.
The first coin Nibbles snatched up bent between his fingers.
"Hang on," he said. "This is rubbish."
The goblins howled with laughter.
"Fake gold!" one cried.
Another fired an arrow.
The arrow thudded into the road beside Dave's foot.
Dave squeaked, raised both hands, and shouted the first spell that came into his head.
A potato shot from his fingertips.
It flew through the air with surprising speed and struck the nearest goblin squarely between the eyes.
The goblin dropped backwards off the log.
Everyone stared.
Even the other goblins stared.
Dave blinked. "That worked."
Thorn did not waste the moment. With a roar, he charged.
Loki charged with him, barking furiously. Derek lifted his shield and moved after them, muttering a prayer under his breath. Nibbles threw the fake coin away in disgust, drew his blade and vanished towards the side of the road, slipping between the trees.
The fight was short, loud and extremely confusing.
Goblins darted from bush to bush, firing arrows, swinging blades and shouting insults that were not nearly as clever as they seemed to think. Thorn drove them back with heavy swings of his axe. Derek stood firm whenever they tried to rush the group, his shield ringing as blades glanced off it. Nibbles appeared from behind one goblin, jabbed it sharply, then disappeared again before it could complain properly.
Dave fired another potato.
This one hit a tree.
The tree did not seem badly hurt.
Soon the goblins' laughter turned to panic. One threw down its sword and bolted into the forest. Another followed. A third, seeing Thorn and Loki bearing down on him together, made a noise like a kettle and fled north along a narrow track.
"They're running!" cried Nibbles.
"After them!" said Thorn.
"Is that sensible?" asked Derek.
"No," said Nibbles.
They chased them anyway.
The goblin trail led away from the road, through brambles, over roots and up a muddy slope. They passed scraps of torn cloth, little footprints in the wet earth and, once, a broken shield marked with the symbol of Fallcrest.
Derek picked it up and frowned. "This belonged to someone from the town."
"Then the goblins have been busy," said Thorn.
Loki sniffed the shield, then the ground, then barked sharply towards the slope.
Beyond the trees, half-hidden by ivy and stone, yawned the mouth of a cave.
The goblins had gone inside.
For a moment, the companions stood outside the entrance and listened. From within came faint echoes: scurrying feet, goblin voices and something that might have been crying.
"We stay together," Derek said firmly.
"Obviously," said Nibbles.
A few minutes later, they split up.
It was not anyone's best idea.
The cave twisted almost immediately into several narrow passages. One sloped down into darkness. Another bent sharply to the left. A third smelled of smoke, mushrooms and old socks.
"We can cover more ground this way," said Nibbles.
"That is exactly what people say before something eats them," Derek replied.
But the goblins were scattering, and someone was still crying deeper in the cave. There was no time to argue properly.
Thorn and Loki took the passage with the freshest tracks. Derek followed the sound of movement to the left. Dave, after hesitating, crept towards the smoky tunnel. Nibbles chose the darkest passage, because it looked the most interesting and therefore probably had treasure in it.
The cave swallowed them one by one.
Thorn moved quickly, trusting Loki's nose. The guard dog padded ahead, sniffing every crack and corner. The tunnel opened into a rough chamber full of broken crates, old bones and stolen junk. At the far end, a goblin wearing a bent cooking pot as a helmet was trying to drag a sack behind a rock.
Thorn raised his axe.
The goblin raised both hands.
"Not boss!" it squealed.
"Where is your boss?" Thorn growled.
The goblin pointed in three directions at once.
Loki growled.
The goblin pointed properly.
Elsewhere, Derek found two goblins arguing over a dented helmet.
"It is mine!" snapped one.
"You don't have a head big enough!" snapped the other.
Derek cleared his throat.
Both goblins turned.
The dwarf sighed. "Put it down."
They did not put it down.
A moment later, one goblin was running away without the helmet, and the other was stuck headfirst inside it.
Dave's tunnel led him to a smoky little firepit. A goblin sentry sat beside it, roasting something on a stick. When it saw Dave, it froze.
Dave froze too.
The goblin looked at Dave.
Dave looked at the goblin.
The goblin looked at the potato that had suddenly appeared in Dave's hand.
"No," said the goblin.
"Sorry," said Dave.
The potato flew.
Nibbles, meanwhile, had discovered treasure.
At least, he had discovered a box.
The box was locked, which meant it was definitely treasure. He knelt beside it, pulled out his tools and began working at the lock with great seriousness.
Click.
The lid opened.
Inside were three buttons, a dead beetle and another handful of fake gold.
Nibbles stared at it.
"I hate goblins," he said.
Then he heard the crying again.
This time it was close.
The companions reached the central chamber from different tunnels almost at the same moment.
It was larger than the others, with a low ceiling and crude torches jammed between cracks in the stone. Stolen packs lay scattered across the floor. A rough wooden cage stood against the far wall.
Inside the cage was a young woman with dirt on her face, torn sleeves and a fierce expression.
She looked up as the companions entered.
"You're not goblins," she said.
"No," said Dave.
"Mostly not," said Nibbles, glancing at Thorn.
Thorn ignored him. "Are you hurt?"
"I'll live," she said. "My name is Mara. I was carrying a message to Fallcrest when those little monsters ambushed me."
"Was the message important?" asked Derek.
Mara hesitated.
Before she could answer, a furious voice screeched from the shadows.
"Mine! Prisoner mine! Shiny bag mine!"
The goblin boss burst from behind a pile of crates.
He was taller than the others, though only slightly, and wore a ragged red cloak that had clearly once been a curtain. In one hand he held a rusty sword. In the other he clutched a small, wriggling rat with patchy fur and a long twitching nose.
"My cave!" shrieked the boss. "My prisoner! My rat!"
The rat squeaked.
Loki's ears shot up.
The goblin boss saw the dog looking.
"No," he said.
Loki barked.
The rat panicked.
It wriggled free, dropped to the floor and shot across the chamber like a furry arrow.
Loki went after it.
"Not Ratbag!" howled the goblin boss.
The chamber erupted.
Thorn charged the boss. Derek rushed to Mara's cage. Dave lifted both hands, trying to aim around everyone. Nibbles slipped behind the crates, looking for the perfect moment to strike.
Loki chased Ratbag under a broken table, around a pile of sacks, between Dave's legs and straight over the goblin boss's foot.
The boss yelped.
Thorn swung.
The goblin ducked.
Dave fired a potato.
The potato hit the red cloak and burst into mash.
Nibbles appeared from nowhere and stabbed the boss in the backside.
The goblin screamed so loudly that dust fell from the ceiling.
Meanwhile, Loki had cornered Ratbag beside a cracked clay pot. There was one final squeak, one triumphant bark, and the goblin boss turned just in time to see his beloved rat meet a heroic but unfortunate end.
"Ratbag!" he wailed.
That was the last mistake he made.
Thorn knocked the rusty sword from his hand. Derek smashed the cage lock with his hammer, and Mara stumbled free. Nibbles kicked the goblin's legs from under him, and Dave, with great concentration, summoned one final potato.
It struck the goblin boss on the forehead.
He collapsed flat on his back.
The cave went quiet.
Loki trotted back to Thorn looking extremely pleased with himself.
Nibbles looked at the fallen goblin boss, then at the dog.
"Remind me never to own a rat," he said.
Mara leaned against the broken cage, breathing hard.
"Thank you," she said. "All of you."
Derek checked her injuries while Thorn searched the chamber. Dave carefully avoided stepping on any suspicious mushrooms. Nibbles searched for real treasure and, after several disappointments, found enough coins to improve his mood slightly.
Mara retrieved a leather satchel from beneath a pile of stolen cloaks. She clutched it tightly to her chest.
"That's what they wanted?" Thorn asked.
"I don't think they knew what it was," Mara said. "But someone did."
She opened the satchel just enough for them to glimpse the edge of old parchment inside. Strange marks covered it: lines, symbols and shapes that looked far older than any ordinary map.
"I was taking this to Fallcrest," she said. "To the wizard Nimozaran."
Derek's eyebrows rose. "The wizard?"
Mara nodded. "Or at least to his apprentice. It has to reach him."
"Then we'll get you there," Thorn said.
Mara studied him for a moment, then smiled.
"I believe you."
Leaving the cave took longer than entering it, mostly because Nibbles insisted on checking every box, sack and crack in the wall for treasure. Most of what he found was useless. One sack contained turnips. Another contained socks. A third contained something that moved, so he wisely left it alone.
At last they emerged into daylight.
The forest seemed brighter than before. The danger had passed, at least for now. Behind them, the goblin cave lay quiet. Ahead, beyond the trees and the long curve of the North Road, waited Fallcrest.
Mara looked tired, but she walked with purpose.
"When we reach town," she said, "come to the Blue Moon Alehouse. I owe you a proper reward. Food, drink, coin if I can arrange it."
"Food?" said Dave.
"Drink?" said Derek.
"Coin?" said Nibbles.
"Loki likes sausages," said Thorn.
Mara laughed for the first time since they had found her.
"I'll see what I can do."
So the companions returned to the North Road: Thorn Axehand, Derek the cleric, Dave the potato wizard, Nibbles McPicklepants and Loki, slayer of Ratbag.
They had left that road as wandering adventurers.
They returned as heroes of the North Road.
And far away, though none of them knew it yet, old magic stirred beneath ancient stone.


# Chapter Two
# The Blue Moon Alehouse
By the time the companions left the forest behind, the afternoon sun was beginning to sink towards the western hills.
The dark trees gave way to rolling farmland divided by dry stone walls and hedgerows. Sheep grazed peacefully in green fields while farmers waved as wagons rattled along the road. After the dangers of the goblin cave, it felt almost impossible that the world could still be so calm.
Mara slowed her pace and smiled.
"We're nearly there."
Dave looked hopefully ahead.
"Fallcrest?"
She nodded.
"You'll see it in a moment."
The road curved gently around a small hill.
Then Fallcrest appeared.
The River Nentir swept around the southern edge of the town, its broad waters alive with barges and fishing boats. Long wooden docks stretched out into the river where merchants shouted orders while sailors unloaded timber, barrels, grain and crates from boats arriving from the south.
Beyond the docks rose the town itself.
A great cliff split Fallcrest in two.
Below the cliff lay Lowtown, where busy streets wound between warehouses, workshops and homes built amongst the remains of much older buildings. Some walls were blackened with age. Others had been rebuilt from fresh timber. Here and there broken towers and ruined foundations reminded everyone that, a hundred years before, an orc army had reached Fallcrest's walls.
Above the cliff stood Upper Fallcrest.
Stone houses overlooked the valley below, protected by strong walls that had survived the invasion. A broad staircase climbed the face of the cliff, linking the two halves of the town together.
Above everything else stood a tall stone tower.
Dave stopped walking.
"That's Nimozaran's tower."
It wasn't a question.
Mara smiled.
"It is."
"I thought it would be bigger."
Nibbles looked up.
"I thought it would have skulls."
"It probably has books instead," Derek said.
"Worse," muttered Nibbles.

The guards at the town gate recognised Mara immediately.
"Mara!"
One of them hurried forwards.
"We'd almost given up hope."
"The goblins nearly did," Mara admitted.
She stepped aside.
"But these four found me before it was too late."
The guard removed his helmet respectfully.
"Then Fallcrest owes you all a debt."
Thorn shook his head.
"We were happy to help."
Nibbles nudged Dave.
"I would've accepted a debt."
Dave whispered back,
"I think that's called money."
The gates swung open.
"Welcome to Fallcrest."

The town was louder than any of them expected.
Carts rattled over cobbled streets.
Blacksmiths hammered glowing iron.
Children laughed as they chased one another through the market square.
The smell of fresh bread drifted from a bakery while somewhere nearby someone was roasting meat over an open fire.
Loki's nose twitched furiously.
Within moments he had discovered three fascinating smells, two friendly dogs and one butcher who decided such a magnificent animal deserved half a sausage.
"Loki," Thorn called.
The dog looked up.
The sausage disappeared in one bite.
Thorn sighed.
"I suppose you've earned it."

Mara led them through the bustling streets of Lowtown.
"Most visitors stay down here," she explained.
"The river brings traders from all over the Nentir Vale."
She pointed towards the docks.
"Everything comes through Fallcrest eventually."
"And the ruins?" Thorn asked.
Mara glanced towards a roofless stone building standing beside a newly built warehouse.
"The orcs destroyed most of Lowtown a century ago."
She rested a hand on one of the old stone walls as they walked past.
"My grandfather helped rebuild this street."
Thorn looked around.
Everywhere old stone met new timber.
The town wasn't pretending the past had never happened.
It had simply carried on.

The broad staircase leading to Upper Fallcrest climbed the cliff in long zigzags.
By the time they reached the top, Dave was breathing heavily.
"Do people... walk... up here... every day?"
"They do," Mara replied cheerfully.
"They're all showing off."
From the top of the cliff the whole valley stretched before them.
The River Nentir curved away towards the south.
Beyond the farms lay dark forests.
Beyond the forests stretched wide open plains.
Far away, almost lost in the afternoon haze, Thorn noticed a great shelf of land rising above the horizon.
It looked too straight to be natural.
He frowned.
"What's that?"
Mara followed his gaze.
"Oh..."
"The old plateau."
"Nobody goes there much."
"Why not?" Dave asked.
She shrugged.
"There are easier places to visit."
Something about the distant rise caught Thorn's attention.
He couldn't explain why.

The Nentir Inn stood close to the centre of Upper Fallcrest.
Unlike the noisy taverns below, it was warm, clean and welcoming.
The innkeeper, Wisara, hurried around the counter the moment she saw Mara.
"You made it!"
She hugged her tightly before noticing the companions.
"I owe them my life," Mara said.
Wisara smiled warmly.
"Then tonight they stay here."
She handed Thorn a small ring of keys.
"Four rooms."
"No charge."
Dave blinked.
"Actual rooms?"
"With beds."
"Soft beds?"
"The softest."
Nibbles folded his arms.
"I don't trust free things."
Wisara laughed.
"Then don't eat the free breakfast."
Nibbles looked horrified.
"There's breakfast too?"

After washing away the dust of the road, the companions spent the afternoon exploring.
Thorn wandered towards the blacksmith's forge.
The rhythmic clang of hammer on steel echoed into the street.
The blacksmith herself was a broad-shouldered woman with soot-blackened arms and a smile that suggested she enjoyed difficult work.
"What can I do for you?"
"I'd like an axe."
She raised an eyebrow.
"I've got plenty."
"I mean..."
Thorn hesitated.
"I'd like my axe."
Understanding spread across her face.
"Ah."
"The sort you keep for a lifetime."
He nodded.
She studied him for a moment before disappearing into the workshop.
When she returned, she carried a piece of chalk and began sketching ideas onto a slate board.
Head shapes.
Haft lengths.
Balance points.
By the time Thorn left, he wasn't carrying a new axe.
But he knew exactly what it would look like when it was finished.

Meanwhile, Dave had discovered a tiny shop overflowing with books, scrolls and magical supplies.
The elderly shopkeeper peered over tiny spectacles.
"Wizard?"
Dave smiled proudly.
"I'm learning."
"Good."
"We need more young wizards."
Dave explained he wanted magical ink for copying spells into his spellbook.
She produced a tiny crystal bottle filled with shimmering blue ink.
"It isn't cheap."
Dave counted his coins twice before carefully handing them over.
As she wrapped the bottle in soft cloth she smiled.
"Use it wisely."
"I will."
"And don't spill it."
Dave looked suddenly nervous.
"I hadn't thought of that."

Nibbles' afternoon was rather less successful.
"I'd like to buy poison," he announced confidently.
The merchant looked him up and down.
"What sort?"
"The sneaky sort."
She produced a tiny green vial.
"Twenty-five gold."
Nibbles almost dropped it.
"For this little thing?"
"It is very good poison."
"I don't need very good poison."
She slowly took the bottle back.
"I think perhaps..."
"...you don't need poison."
Nibbles sighed dramatically.
"I'll come back when I'm richer."
"I hope you don't."
Nibbles wasn't entirely sure whether that had been polite.
As he wandered back towards the market square, he decided that becoming richer had suddenly become a very important goal.

Derek spent his afternoon doing something that none of the others had thought to do.
He asked questions.
While Thorn dreamed about axes, Dave worried about spilling ink, and Nibbles considered the unfair price of poison, Derek spoke with stallholders, guards and passing townsfolk.
He asked about the road.
He asked about goblins.
He asked about Fallcrest.
That was how he met Sergeant Murgaddin.
The old dwarf stood near the market square wearing battered armour and a helmet with a dent above one eye. His grey beard was neatly braided, and his expression suggested he had seen every kind of trouble the world could offer and was still disappointed by most of it.
"So," Murgaddin said, looking Derek up and down.
"You're one of the youngsters who pulled Mara out of a goblin cave."
Derek nodded.
"With my friends."
"Good answer."
Murgaddin glanced across the square, where Nibbles was attempting to look innocent beside a stall of shiny belt buckles.
"Are they always like that?"
"Usually worse."
The sergeant's moustache twitched.
"Then Fallcrest may be in trouble."
He led Derek back towards the others and introduced himself properly.
"I keep order around here," Murgaddin said.
"Or I try to."
Thorn straightened.
"We don't want trouble."
"Trouble rarely cares what people want."
Murgaddin looked towards Mara, who had rejoined them near the stairway.
"She says you can be trusted."
Mara nodded.
"They can."
"That matters," said the sergeant.
"Fallcrest remembers people who help."
Nibbles brightened.
"Does it remember them with money?"
"It remembers them with opportunities."
Nibbles' face fell.
"That sounds like less money."
Murgaddin gave him a long look.
"I'll keep an eye on you."
"Thank you?"
"That was not a compliment."

By evening, the lamps of Fallcrest were being lit.
The companions followed Mara back down the cliff steps into Lowtown, where the river air smelled of wet rope, woodsmoke and supper.
The Blue Moon Alehouse stood near the docks at the edge of a busy square. Its timber beams leaned slightly, its windows glowed warmly, and its painted sign showed a cheerful blue moon grinning down at everyone who passed.
Music spilled from inside.
So did laughter.
"This," said Mara, "is where people come when they want to forget the day."
"Is the food good?" Dave asked.
"Very."
"Then I also want to forget the day."
Inside, the Blue Moon was crowded but welcoming.
Sailors drank beside merchants. Workers from the docks argued over dice. A travelling musician played a lively tune near the hearth. The innkeeper waved when she saw Mara and pointed them towards a large table already laid with food.
There was fresh bread.
Cheese.
Roast chicken.
A thick vegetable stew.
And apple pie.
Dave stared at the table.
"This is more beautiful than magic."
Derek sat heavily.
"I may agree with you."
Mara laughed and began serving everyone before taking anything for herself.
She made sure Dave had enough chicken, asked Derek about his travels, listened while Thorn described the axe he had commissioned, and smuggled pieces of sausage to Loki beneath the table.
Thorn noticed.
Again.
He said nothing.
Loki sat beside Mara as if he had always belonged there.
Nibbles explained his failed poison purchase in great detail.
"The problem," he said, "is that merchants don't understand ambition."
"The problem," Derek replied, "is that poison is expensive because people use it to poison people."
"Exactly," said Nibbles.
"Unfair."
For a while, nobody spoke of goblins.
Nobody spoke of maps.
Nobody spoke of danger.
They ate until Dave declared he could not move another inch, then proved himself wrong when Mara offered him more pie.

The shouting began outside.
At first it sounded like ordinary children playing in the square.
Then one voice rose above the others.
"It's back!"
Another joined it.
"The little statue!"
"On the roof again!"
Nibbles looked up immediately.
"Statue?"
Thorn reached across the table without looking.
"Don't steal it."
"I wasn't going to steal it."
"Don't climb onto the roof either."
Nibbles paused.
"I wasn't going to climb very high."
He slipped outside before anyone could stop him.
Several children stood in the square pointing towards the roof of the Blue Moon. One small boy had both hands cupped around his mouth.
"Oi! Stone thing!"
"Come back!"
Nibbles shaded his eyes and looked up.
At first he saw only roof tiles and chimneys.
Then something moved.
A tiny grey shape crouched beside one of the chimney pots.
It was no bigger than a cat, with thin limbs and a hunched back. For a moment it looked exactly like a carved gargoyle.
Then it turned its head.
Nibbles froze.
The creature had wings.
Not feathered wings.
Stone-coloured wings, folded tight against its back.
Its little eyes glinted.
Nibbles took one careful step towards the wall.
The creature saw him.
Its wings snapped open.
With a single silent leap, it launched itself from the rooftop, glided over the square and vanished into the shadows between two taller buildings.
The children cheered.
Nibbles did not.
He hurried back inside.
"There was definitely something up there."
Derek put down his spoon.
"What sort of something?"
"A statue."
"Statues don't fly," Dave said.
"This one did."
Thorn frowned.
"Did it see you?"
Nibbles considered lying.
Then remembered Thorn usually noticed.
"Yes."
Mara's smile faded slightly.
"That's... odd."
Before anyone could say more, the tavern door opened.
A halfling rushed inside carrying a stack of papers, two rolled scrolls, a leather satchel, several loose quills and what appeared to be half a biscuit.
He was cheerful, breathless and untidy all at once.
"Mara!"
He hurried across the room.
"You made it!"
Mara stood and hugged him.
"Only just."
The halfling turned towards the companions.
"Then you must be the heroes."
He beamed.
"I'm Tobbs."
"Tobbs Quickfoot."
"Apprentice to the great Nimozaran, keeper of too many keys, fetcher of too many books, and currently very glad to meet you."
He shook Thorn's hand with both of his.
Then Derek's.
Then Dave's.
Then Nibbles'.
Then he noticed Loki.
"Ah."
He crouched and offered one hand.
"And you must be the famous dog."
Loki gave him a serious sniff.
Apparently satisfied, he allowed Tobbs to shake his paw.
"Excellent manners," Tobbs said.
