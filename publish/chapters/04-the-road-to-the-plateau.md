The river road east of Fallcrest was quieter than the tracks around town. Farms thinned to hedgerows, hedgerows to open grass, and grass at last to wild country where the trees grew thicker and the wind carried the smell of wet stone from far hills. Thorn had the Ethium Stone wrapped in cloth at his belt, the manor mage's letter folded away in his pack, and Tobbs's map copied onto oiled paper where he could reach it without stopping.

They made good time on the first day. Loki ranged ahead through the long grass. Dave asked questions about the plateau every twenty minutes. Nibbles complained that nobody had yet invented a horse that did not smell. Derek walked at the centre of the group with the calm patience of someone who had learned long ago that roads were where trouble usually appeared.

By evening they camped in a hollow above the river, where the water could be heard but not seen through a screen of willows. Derek built the fire small and neat. Thorn took first watch, then Dave, who kept starting at every owl and apologising to the darkness.

The wolves came in the deep hours before dawn, when Derek was on watch.

He saw them first as movement at the edge of the firelight — low shapes, too coordinated for stray dogs. Three of them, maybe four, circling with the careful hunger of animals that had learned travellers meant food.

"Derek?" Thorn was awake before the second shout was needed.

The wolves charged.

It was not a long fight, but it was ugly. Thorn met the first with his axe and drove it back yelping into the dark. Loki seized another by the scruff and shook it until it stopped trying to be brave. Dave, half out of his bedroll, hurled a potato at the third wolf with such force of surprise that it bounced off its shoulder and the creature fled altogether. The last wolf lunged at Nibbles, who had drawn both blades and was offering a detailed critique of its life choices; Derek's hammer caught it across the ribs and it too ran.

Silence returned by degrees. The fire popped. Somewhere far off, a wolf howled once, as if making a note for later.

"Well," Nibbles said, sheathing his daggers. "I'm definitely awake."

They did not sleep soundly after that.

---

The second day brought older country.

Burial mounds rose from the grass like green-backed whales — ancient, turf-covered, marked here and there with stones worn smooth by centuries of rain. The track wound between them, and for a long while nothing happened except birdsong and the creak of packs on shoulders.

Then Loki stopped.

His hackles rose. Thorn stopped too. A moment later they all heard it — a harsh, carrying cry from beyond the nearest mound, and then another, and then the thunder of many feet.

"Axe beaks," Derek said quietly.

They came over the rise in a rush — tall flightless birds with legs like stilts and beaks like hatchets, six or seven of them, territorial and furious and far faster than anything that size had a right to be. The herd swept down the slope with their necks stretched forward and their beaks snapping.

Thorn planted himself in their path. Derek raised his shield. Dave shouted the words of the Sleep spell he had copied in the manor cellar — and for a wonderful, improbable moment, the two leading axe beaks folded up in the grass as though someone had cut their strings.

The others did not sleep.

The fight became a scramble of wings, dust and shouting. Nibbles darted between the birds' legs, stabbing at soft places. Thorn's axe rang off bone. An axe beak knocked Dave sideways into a mound; he sat down hard, blinked, and produced a potato with the grim determination of a wizard who had run out of better ideas. Loki barked and snapped and was altogether too brave for his own good until Thorn hauled him back by the collar.

When it was over, three axe beaks fled limping across the hills and the rest did not get up.

The birds were enormous. Nibbles looked from one fallen axe beak to another, then down at their legs.

"Those," he said, "are drumsticks."

No one argued. Each leg was nearly as long as Dave's forearm — jointed, muscular, and far too much meat to leave behind. Thorn and Derek set to with axe and belt knife while Dave studied the burial mounds with great attention and Loki watched every cut with professional interest. They packed what they could carry, greasy bundles strapped to their packs, enough for several meals if the smell did not bring more trouble down on them.

"Note to self," Nibbles said, leaning on his knee and breathing hard. "Avoid large angry birds."

"Note to self," Dave echoed, "Sleep works on birds."

"Sometimes," Derek said.

---

They camped that night among the burial mounds, where the old stones caught the last of the sunset and the air smelled of grass and earth and something older than either. Nibbles roasted axe beak leg over the fire until the outside crackled — a drumstick the size of a small ham — and divided it with the seriousness of a quartermaster. Derek volunteered for the middle watch.

He liked the quiet. He liked knowing where everyone was. He sat with his back to a mound and his hammer across his knees, watching the fire settle to embers.

At first he thought the shape near the eastern stones was another mound — a darker lump against the dark, too still to be animal, too low to be tree.

Then it moved.

Not much. A shift of weight. A hooded head turning slowly towards the camp, as though counting sleeping bodies.

Derek stood.

"Who's there?"

The shape rose — taller than he had thought, cloaked from head to foot — and for one heartbeat they regarded each other across the firelit grass. Then the figure stepped back, melted into the shadow beyond the stones, and was gone so completely that Derek might have doubted his own eyes, except that Loki was already growling low in his throat.

Thorn joined him a moment later, axe in hand. "What is it?"

"Watcher," Derek said. "Cloaked. Didn't want a fight."

"Didn't want us to know they were there, either."

They doubled the watch for the rest of the night. Nothing else came out of the dark. But when dawn broke grey and cold over the mounds, every one of them felt the same thing: they were not the only people on this road.

---

On the third morning the river turned east as Tobbs had promised, and the land began to climb.

The track left the valley and wound into broken hills where trees clung to slopes and the sound of water grew from a murmur to a roar. By midday they could see the plateau — a vast shelf of land above the treeline, too level to be natural, its edge lost in haze. And below it, where the river fell in a white sheet from cliff to cliff, the waterfall.

Even from a distance it looked like a bad place to climb.

"That's the way up," Thorn said, studying Tobbs's map.

"Of course it is," Nibbles said.

The path to the falls was narrow, wet, and partly hidden behind the cascade where the river threw up a constant cold mist. Spray soaked them before they had gone twenty yards. Stone steps, ancient and cracked, threaded up beside the thundering water. Goblins had been using it — that much was obvious from the scrap marks, the smell of smoke, and the crude handholds chipped into places where the old builders had not intended anyone to climb.

"They'll be above us," Derek said.

"They'll be expecting us," Thorn agreed. "So we don't give them what they expect."

They split where the path forked behind the falls — a longer ledge that wound upward with more handholds but less cover, and a steeper scramble to the right where the rock was slick and the spray fell in sheets.

Thorn took the longer route with Dave and Loki. Nibbles looked at the steep way, looked at Thorn, and said, "I'll take the interesting one."

"Of course you will," Thorn said.

Derek went with him.

---

Arrows hissed out of the spray almost at once.

Goblins crouched on ledges above the climb — small shapes with bows, shouting to one another in high voices that the waterfall almost swallowed. One arrow skittered off Thorn's shield. Another struck the rock near Dave's foot and vanished into the mist below.

Dave raised both hands, remembered the manor, remembered the axe beaks, and shouted the Sleep spell with everything he had.

Two goblin archers went limp and slid sideways off their perches into the ferns. A third dropped his bow in astonishment. Loki barked triumphantly.

"Again!" Thorn yelled. "Do it again!"

"I can't — not yet — I need —"

"Then duck!"

A bugbear came down the path like a landslide — huge, shaggy, carrying a morningstar that looked as though it had been stolen from someone larger. Thorn met it on the narrow ledge with his axe and the wall of the cliff at his back. Water hammered down inches from his shoulder. Spray filled the air until he could barely see the creature's face.

It was, he thought later, the sort of fight that would either make a good story or a very short obituary.

The bugbear swung. Thorn blocked, gave ground, blocked again. The ledge was slick. The roar of the falls was so loud he could not hear his own breath. Loki darted in and snapped at the bugbear's ankle; the creature roared and kicked, missed, and Thorn used the moment to drive his shoulder into its chest and push it sideways into the torrent where the water beat the stone.

The bugbear did not fall — but it slipped, swore in a language Thorn did not know, and gave him room for one clean swing that knocked the fight out of it.

Above on the steep route, Nibbles had his own problems.

The second bugbear had come down the right-hand scramble where the stone was steepest and the goblins had not bothered to guard because nobody sensible would climb there. Nibbles had climbed there anyway, because Nibbles considered sensible a word used by people who missed treasure.

He fought well — quick, vicious, swearing with every strike. The bugbear fought better. It caught him a blow across the ribs that knocked the breath from his body and sent him skidding on wet rock. Derek healed him once, pulling him back from the edge with a hand on his collar. Nibbles got up, swore again, and went back in. The bugbear hit him again. Derek healed him again, jaw tight.

"Stay down this time," Derek said.

"I am not staying down." Nibbles wiped blood from his mouth. "I am going to kill that thing."

He did, on the third rush — a lucky strike under the bugbear's arm while Derek's hammer took it from the other side. The creature toppled into the spray and did not get up.

At the top of the climb, a goblin tried to run.

Dave, who had just reached the upper ledge with Thorn and was still shaking from the fight, pointed one finger and spoke the first word of a spell he had practised a hundred times in his head and only now tried in anger. Fire leapt from his hand — a bolt of orange light that cracked through the mist and struck the fleeing goblin square between the shoulders. The creature pitched forward and did not move again.

Dave stared at his own hand.

"...That worked too."

Thorn clapped him on the arm hard enough to hurt. "Come on. Up."

They gathered at the cliff top with the plateau opening before them and the waterfall thundering behind. Everyone was bruised, wet, and breathing hard. Nibbles sat down immediately and declared he was never climbing again. Loki shook himself and sprayed everyone.

But they were up.

---

The Ethium plateau was nothing like the forests below.

Stone foundations stretched across the high ground beneath creeping vines and wind-bent grass. Roads led to walls that had fallen centuries ago. Tobbs had called the whole plateau Ethium, and walking it now Thorn believed him — not one ruined town but the bones of a city so vast that rubble showed in every direction. Whatever had stood here had died long before Fallcrest was rebuilt, and the silence was the silence of a place that remembered being important.

They walked for the rest of the afternoon without speaking much. The air was thinner. The light had a strange clarity, as though the plateau sharpened everything it touched. Once, far to the east, Nibbles thought he saw goblin shapes on wolfback racing along a distant ridge — fast, organised, hunting something that was not them. He pointed. The riders vanished behind a fold of land.

"Not our problem," Thorn said.

"Yet," Derek murmured.

---

They made camp as the sun sank towards the western hills.

Dave was the first to see the dragon.

He stopped walking so abruptly that Nibbles walked into the back of his legs. "What —"

"Look."

A shape cut across the clouds far above the plateau — at first no larger than a bird, then impossibly larger. Wings like torn shadows. A long tail trailing behind it. Black against the evening sky. It circled once, slow and terrible, as though surveying ground it already owned.

Nobody spoke.

The dragon banked eastward. Something slipped from its jaws — a dark shape, small against the sky — and tumbled down towards the broken hills below.

"Was that—" Dave began.

Nobody wanted to finish the sentence.

The dragon slid away into the haze above the broken hills.

Dave swallowed. "I always said adventures should have more dragons."

"I assume you didn't mean the sort that's that big?" Nibbles said quietly.

They watched the sky for a long time after it was gone. Nothing else moved up there. But the image stayed — vast, casual, indifferent to anything walking below.

---

Tobbs had marked a collapsed shrine on their map, a little way north of the waterfall path — a safe place, he had said, if the stones on the plateau were waking. They set out towards it as the light failed, picking their way through scrub and fallen stone while the last colour drained from the sky.

They found the shrine just beyond a low rise: a small stone building half-sunken into the earth, its roof fallen in, its walls cracked but still standing. Pale near-white blocks — Ethium work, smooth as marble — framed the altar at the centre, where smaller worked stones were set, faintly warm to the touch.

Derek rested his palm on one and felt a pulse like a slow heartbeat.

"Ethium work," he said.

"Good work or bad work?" Nibbles asked.

"Old work."

They made camp inside the shrine walls, where the wind could not reach and the night felt oddly sheltered — as though the place remembered being a refuge. When they woke in the morning, bruises had faded a little, breath came easier, and even Nibbles admitted his ribs hurt less than they should.

"Shrine healing," Derek said. "I'll take it."

They ate, packed, and walked on.

The morning was clear, and the plateau opened around them with every mile. Ahead, the land climbed to its highest point — a great hill ringed with cliffs that dropped away on three sides, too vast and too deliberate to be natural. Against that skyline stood the ruins of a castle: blackened walls, broken battlements, and the jagged outline of what might once have been a palace. Nobody in Fallcrest could have seen this from the cliffs. You had to be walking Ethium's bones to know it was there.

Dave stopped. "That's the biggest thing left standing."

"And not where we're going," Thorn said, checking Tobbs's map. The castle crowned the largest hill on the plateau — older and grander than anything they had passed, though none of them could have said who had built it or why. Their destination lay elsewhere, on a smaller ridge to the west, marked in blue ink where the ruined tower waited.

"We're not going up there," he added. "Not yet."

They turned west, keeping the great hill at their backs.

The ruined tower lay somewhere ahead on Tobbs's map — a ridge marked in blue ink, west of the great hill and well away from the waterfall path they had climbed. Thorn led the way with the map folded in his hand. Dave kept glancing at the sky. Nibbles complained that his boots were still damp from the waterfall and that nobody had warned him Ethium would be so large. Derek walked at the centre of the group, calm as ever, and Loki trotted ahead with his nose to the wind.

Behind them, the castle hill and its blackened ruins sank slowly into the haze. Before them, the tower waited — or so Tobbs's blue mark promised. They had come too far to turn back now.

They set out towards it.
