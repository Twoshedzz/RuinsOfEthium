By the time the companions left the forest behind, the afternoon sun was beginning to sink towards the western hills. The dark trees gave way to rolling farmland divided by dry stone walls and hedgerows, where sheep grazed peacefully in green fields and farmers waved as wagons rattled along the road. After the dangers of the goblin cave, it felt almost impossible that the world could still be so calm.

Mara slowed her pace and smiled at the road ahead. "We're nearly there," she said. "You'll see Fallcrest in a moment."

The road curved gently around a small hill, and then the town appeared.

The River Nentir swept around the southern edge of Fallcrest, its broad waters alive with barges and fishing boats. Long wooden docks stretched out into the current where merchants shouted orders and sailors unloaded timber, barrels, grain and crates from boats arriving from the south. Beyond the docks rose the town itself, split in two by a great cliff of pale stone.

Below the cliff lay Lowtown — busy streets winding between warehouses, workshops and homes built amongst the remains of much older buildings. Some walls were blackened with age. Others had been rebuilt from fresh timber. Here and there broken towers and ruined foundations reminded everyone that, a hundred years before, an orc army had reached Fallcrest's walls and left scars the town had never quite erased.

Above the cliff stood Upper Fallcrest, where stone houses overlooked the valley below, protected by strong walls that had survived the invasion. A broad staircase climbed the face of the cliff in long zigzags, linking the two halves of the town together. And above everything else, rising from the heart of the upper town, stood a tall stone tower with narrow windows and a pointed roof.

Dave stopped walking and stared up at it. "That's Nimozaran's tower," he said. It was not quite a question.

Mara nodded. "It is."

"I thought it would be bigger," Dave admitted.

Nibbles looked up beside him. "I thought it would have skulls."

"Probably books instead," Derek said.

"Worse," muttered Nibbles.

Stone walls and a northern gate rose ahead of them as the road descended towards the town. Banners snapped in the wind above Knight's Gate, and beyond them came the sound of hammers, voices and rushing water far below. The guards on duty recognised Mara at once.

"Mara!" one of them called, hurrying forward. "We'd almost given up hope."

"The goblins nearly got me," she admitted, stepping aside so the companions could be seen properly. "These four found me before it was too late."

The guard removed his helmet respectfully. "Then Fallcrest owes you all a debt."

Thorn shook his head. "We were happy to help."

Nibbles nudged Dave and whispered that he would have accepted a debt. Dave whispered back that he thought that was called money. The gates swung open anyway.

"Welcome to Fallcrest."

Inside the walls, the town was louder than any of them had expected. Carts rattled over cobbled streets. Blacksmiths hammered glowing iron. Children laughed as they chased one another through the market square, and the smell of fresh bread drifted from a bakery while somewhere nearby someone roasted meat over an open fire.

Loki's nose twitched furiously. Within moments he had discovered three fascinating smells, two friendly dogs and one butcher who decided such a magnificent animal deserved half a sausage.

"Loki," Thorn called.

The dog looked up. The sausage disappeared in one bite.

Thorn sighed. "I suppose you've earned it."

Mara led them through the bustling streets of Lowtown, explaining as they walked that Fallcrest was Almenor's northernmost outpost — the last proper town before the wild mountains and forests took over. Most visitors stayed down here, close to the river, where barges brought trade up from the south and wagons from the west eventually found their way in along the long road over the hills. In the Nentir Vale, she said, almost everything passed through Fallcrest sooner or later — timber from the forests, grain from the farms, goods and gossip from the cities far to the south. When Thorn asked about the ruins, she glanced towards a roofless stone building standing beside a newly built warehouse.

"The orcs destroyed most of Lowtown a century ago," she said quietly. She rested a hand on one of the old stone walls as they passed. "My grandfather helped rebuild this street."

Thorn looked around and saw what she meant. Everywhere old stone met new timber. The town was not pretending the past had never happened. It had simply carried on.

They climbed the broad staircase to Upper Fallcrest in long zigzags that left Dave breathing heavily by the time they reached the top. "Do people walk up here every day?" he asked.

"They do," Mara replied cheerfully. "They're all showing off."

From the cliff top the whole valley stretched before them — the River Nentir curving away towards the south, dark forests beyond the farms, and wide open plains beyond that. Far away, almost lost in the afternoon haze, Thorn noticed a great shelf of land rising above the horizon. It looked too straight, too level, to be natural.

He frowned. "What's that?"

Mara followed his gaze. "Oh. The old plateau. Nobody goes there much."

"Why not?" Dave asked.

She shrugged. "There are easier places to visit."

Something about the distant rise caught Thorn's attention, though he could not have said why. It stayed with him as they made their way to the Nentir Inn, which stood close to the centre of Upper Fallcrest — warm, clean and welcoming in a way the noisier taverns below were not.

The innkeeper, Wisara, hurried around the counter the moment she saw Mara. "You made it!" She hugged her tightly before noticing the companions. "I owe them my life," Mara said.

Wisara smiled warmly. "Then tonight they stay here." She handed Thorn a small ring of keys. "Four rooms. No charge."

Dave blinked. "Actual rooms? With beds? Soft beds?"

"The softest," Wisara assured him.

Nibbles folded his arms. "I don't trust free things."

"Then don't eat the free breakfast," Wisara laughed.

Nibbles looked horrified. "There's breakfast too?"

After washing away the dust of the road, the companions spent the afternoon exploring Fallcrest while Mara saw to her own errands. Thorn wandered towards the blacksmith's forge, where the rhythmic clang of hammer on steel echoed into the street. The blacksmith herself was a broad-shouldered woman with soot-blackened arms and a smile that suggested she enjoyed difficult work.

"I'd like an axe," Thorn said.

She raised an eyebrow. "I've got plenty."

"I mean..." Thorn hesitated. "I'd like my axe. The sort you keep for a lifetime."

Understanding spread across her face. She studied him for a moment, disappeared into the workshop, and returned with a piece of chalk. By the time she had finished sketching head shapes, haft lengths and balance points onto a slate board, Thorn was not carrying a new axe — but he knew exactly what it would look like when it was finished.

Nibbles' afternoon began with an inquiry at a narrow apothecary's shop near the docks, where he announced with confidence that he would like to buy poison — the sneaky sort. The apothecary did not even reach for a shelf.

"That isn't something I keep in stock," she said. "If you want the proper stuff, it has to be ordered in through the halfling dock crews. A hundred gold, and you'd still be waiting."

Nibbles stared at her. "A hundred gold?" He had been picturing something one could slip into a pocket, not a sum that would require an actual career in crime.

The apothecary folded her arms. "Take it up with the docks."

Nibbles sighed dramatically, abandoned the idea, and went to find Dave.

Together they discovered Naerumar's Imports, which Mara had marked on a scrap of paper as the place to buy anything worth having in Fallcrest. The shop was quiet, polished, and full of glass cases containing jewellery, carved figures and objects that looked far too expensive to touch. The owner, Orest Naerumar, was a tiefling with impeccable manners and horns polished to a respectable shine. He greeted them as though they were already his favourite customers.

Dave, ducking slightly through the doorway, explained that he was a wizard in training and needed proper ink for copying spells into his spellbook. Orest produced the supplies without fuss — a crystal bottle of shimmering blue ink and the other bits and pieces a careful scribe might need. Dave counted his coins twice, paid up, and looked delighted.

That was when Nibbles decided they had been far too easy to deal with.

"We'll take one of those as well," he said, pointing at something in a case at random. "And that ring. And whatever you've got in the back that's magical and not on display."

Orest smiled politely and said he would be happy to discuss special orders in due course. Dave, catching the mood, added that they were heroes of the North Road and probably deserved a discount. Nibbles said heroes definitely deserved free samples.

Two very large men then entered from a door at the back. They were not quite armed, but they gave the impression of being heavily armed without needing to show it. Their arms were thick. Their silence was pointed.

Dave became very interested in his ink bottle. Nibbles studied the ceiling with great attention.

Orest's smile did not change. "Perhaps we might discuss your requirements on another occasion," he said.

They left Naerumar's Imports considerably more politely than they had entered.

Later in the afternoon the companions met again near Wizard's Gate and considered buying ponies from a paddock run by a cheerful halfling named Lannar Thistleton, who dealt in horses, mules and anything else likely to carry a pack up a lonely road. The prices seemed reasonable. The animals seemed sturdy. For a few minutes it looked as though the party might leave Fallcrest mounted.

Then Loki caught the smell of horses and took an immediate dislike to it.

He growled. He barked. He barked louder. One pony shied. Another tried to bite Nibbles, though Nibbles insisted that was unrelated. Thorn hauled the dog back by his collar while Dave attempted to explain that Loki was usually very friendly, which the horses did not find reassuring. Lannar suggested they might wish to consider their options elsewhere. They left quickly.

Derek spent part of the afternoon doing something none of the others had thought to do. While Thorn dreamed about axes, Dave worried about spilling ink and Nibbles brooded over the unfair price of ambition, Derek spoke with stallholders, guards and passing townsfolk. He asked about the road. He asked about goblins. He asked about Fallcrest. That was how he met Sergeant Murgaddin.

The old dwarf stood near the market square wearing battered armour and a helmet with a dent above one eye. His grey beard was neatly braided, and his expression suggested he had seen every kind of trouble the world could offer and was still disappointed by most of it.

"So," Murgaddin said, looking Derek up and down. "You're one of the youngsters who pulled Mara out of a goblin cave."

"With my friends," Derek replied.

"Good answer." Murgaddin glanced across the square, where Nibbles was attempting to look innocent beside a stall of shiny belt buckles. "Are they always like that?"

"Usually worse."

The sergeant's moustache twitched. "Then Fallcrest may be in trouble." He led Derek back towards the others and introduced himself properly. "I keep order around here. Or I try to."

Thorn straightened. "We don't want trouble."

"Trouble rarely cares what people want." Murgaddin looked towards Mara, who had rejoined them near the stairway. "She says you can be trusted."

Mara nodded. "They can."

"That matters," said the sergeant. "Fallcrest remembers people who help."

Nibbles brightened. "Does it remember them with money?"

"It remembers them with opportunities."

Nibbles' face fell. "That sounds like less money."

Murgaddin gave him a long look. "I'll keep an eye on you."

"Thank you?"

"That was not a compliment."

By evening the lamps of Fallcrest were being lit. The companions followed Mara back down the cliff steps into Lowtown, where the river air smelled of wet rope, woodsmoke and supper. The Blue Moon Alehouse stood near the docks at the edge of a busy square — timber beams leaning slightly, windows glowing warmly, and a painted sign showing a cheerful blue moon grinning down at everyone who passed. Music spilled from inside. So did laughter.

"This is where people come when they want to forget the day," Mara said.

"Is the food good?" Dave asked.

"Very."

"Then I also want to forget the day."

Inside, the Blue Moon was crowded but welcoming. Sailors drank beside merchants. Workers from the docks argued over dice near the hearth. A travelling musician played a lively tune, and the innkeeper waved when she saw Mara and pointed them towards a large table already laid with food — fresh bread, cheese, roast chicken, a thick vegetable stew, and apple pie.

Dave stared at the spread. "This is more beautiful than magic."

Derek sat heavily. "I may agree with you."

Mara laughed and began serving everyone before taking anything for herself. She made sure Dave had enough chicken, asked Derek about his travels, listened while Thorn described the axe he had commissioned, and smuggled pieces of sausage to Loki beneath the table. Thorn noticed. Again. He said nothing. Loki sat beside Mara as if he had always belonged there.

Nibbles explained his failed poison purchase at some length — the hundred gold, the halfling dock crews, the insult of having to order murder by committee. Derek replied that poison was expensive because people used it to poison people. Nibbles said that was exactly the point, and unfair.

For a while nobody spoke of goblins, or maps, or danger. They ate until Dave declared he could not move another inch, then proved himself wrong when Mara offered him more pie.

The shouting began outside.

At first it sounded like ordinary children playing in the square. Then one voice rose above the others — "It's back!" — and another joined it: "The little statue! On the roof again!"

Derek looked up sharply.

Thorn reached across the table without looking at Nibbles. "Don't steal it."

"I wasn't going to steal it," Nibbles said.

"Don't climb onto the roof either."

Nibbles paused. "I wasn't going to climb very high."

Derek was already on his feet, moving towards the door with the calm urgency of someone who had heard what mattered in the children's voices. Nibbles made a disappointed noise and stayed at the table.

Several children stood in the square pointing towards the roof of the Blue Moon. One small boy had both hands cupped around his mouth and was shouting at the tiles. Derek shaded his eyes and looked up. At first he saw only chimneys and roof beams. Then something moved — a tiny grey shape crouched beside one of the chimney pots, no bigger than a cat, with thin limbs and a hunched back. For a moment it looked exactly like a carved gargoyle.

Then it turned its head.

Derek went still. The creature had wings — not feathered wings, but stone-coloured wings folded tight against its back. Its little eyes glinted. Derek took one careful step towards the wall. The creature saw him, snapped its wings open, and with a single silent leap launched itself from the rooftop. It glided over the square and vanished into the shadows between two taller buildings.

*The Blue Moon roof — where something that was definitely not a statue took flight.*

The children cheered. Derek did not. He turned back inside to report that there had definitely been something up there, and that it had most certainly not been a statue, because statues did not fly.

Thorn frowned. "Did it see you?"

Derek met his gaze. "Yes."

Mara's smile faded slightly. "That's odd."

Before anyone could say more, the tavern door opened and a halfling rushed inside carrying a stack of papers, two rolled scrolls, a leather satchel, several loose quills and what appeared to be half a biscuit. He was cheerful, breathless and untidy all at once.

"Mara! You made it!" He hurried across the room. Mara stood and hugged him. "Only just."

The halfling turned towards the companions and beamed. "Then you must be the heroes. I'm Tobbs. Tobbs Quickfoot. Apprentice to the great Nimozaran, keeper of too many keys, fetcher of too many books, and currently very glad to meet you."

He shook Thorn's hand with both of his, then Derek's, then Dave's, then Nibbles'. When he noticed Loki he crouched and offered one hand. "And you must be the famous dog."

Loki gave him a serious sniff, apparently satisfied, and allowed Tobbs to shake his paw.

"Excellent manners," Tobbs said. "I wish more wizards were like you."

Dave looked offended.

Tobbs sat down at the table, still smiling, still talking, still shuffling papers from one place to another without any obvious reason. But Thorn noticed something the cheerful patter almost hid. The smile never quite reached Tobbs' eyes. Every few moments he glanced towards the window, once towards the door, once towards the ceiling — like someone trying very hard to seem at ease, and almost succeeding.

Mara placed the worn leather satchel on the table. Tobbs stopped moving. For the first time since entering the Blue Moon, he was completely still.

"Is that it?" he asked quietly.

Mara nodded. "I kept it safe."

"I know." His voice softened. "Of course you did."

She opened the satchel and drew out the old parchment. Tobbs unrolled it with great care, and the noisy tavern seemed to fade around them.

The map was older than any map Thorn had ever seen. Its edges were cracked and darkened. Thin lines marked roads that no longer existed, rivers that had changed course and ruins drawn in faded ink. Strange symbols surrounded the outline of a lost city, and several points had been marked in blue. Tobbs stared at them and whispered, "Oh."

"That sounds bad," said Dave.

"It is not bad exactly." Tobbs swallowed. "It is important."

"That's usually worse," Nibbles said.

Tobbs traced one finger gently over the centre of the map. The alehouse seemed to grow quieter, as though the room itself were listening.

"This is Ethium," he said. "Not a town on the plateau — the whole plateau was the city. A vast ancient place, long abandoned, long looted. Or so everyone thought. That faint pale shelf you might have noticed from the cliffs above town, when the haze lifts — that's it. Fallcrest is too far to see what's actually up there. Once it was one of the jewels of the old kingdoms. Roads ran to it from every direction. Wizards studied there. Merchants grew rich. Then the wars came, and the sackings, and for generations people believed there was nothing left but broken walls and stories."

His finger moved to one of the blue marks. "These markings show the locations of Ethium Stones — fragments of the old magic, buried or hidden when the city fell. For centuries they slept. My master, Nimozaran, has spent years collecting every scrap of lore he could find about them, and lately..." He paused, and Thorn saw his hand tighten on the parchment. "Lately his work has stopped being theory."

Tobbs glanced towards the window before continuing. "The stones are awakening. Their magic has been quiet for generations, but recently things have begun to happen. Lights in old ruins where no torches burn. Animals acting strangely near the plateau. People dreaming of places they have never seen — halls of white stone, rivers that run uphill, voices speaking in languages nobody alive should know."

Dave's eyes widened. "That's exciting."

"No," Tobbs said gently. "That's worrying. Because if the stones are waking, someone else will have noticed too. Someone sent goblins after Mara on the North Road. Someone wanted this map badly enough to kill for it."

Mara folded her arms. "Someone sent goblins after me because of this."

"Yes," Tobbs said. "I feared they might."

"And the stones?" Derek asked. "What do they actually do?"

Tobbs looked at him for a moment, then reached into his leather satchel and brought out a small stone cupped in both hands. It was no larger than a hen's egg, smooth and pale grey, with faint blue veins running through it like frozen lightning. In the torchlight of the tavern it looked ordinary. Almost disappointingly so.

"Nimozaran gave me this one," Tobbs said quietly. "There are only a handful in all of Fallcrest, and most of them have been asleep since before our grandparents were born. But they are waking now — and when they wake, they remember what they were made for."

He set the stone on the table between the empty bowls. The companions leaned in. Even Loki sniffed at it once and sat back down, ears pricked.

"The old texts are incomplete," Tobbs continued, "but my master has pieced together a little of the language used to wake them. Not much. A phrase here, a command there. Watch."

He rested two fingers lightly on the stone and spoke two words Thorn had never heard before — clear, careful, almost reverent. "Aethra Lumis."

The change was immediate.

A soft blue light bloomed from within the stone, steady and bright enough to read by, filling the space between their faces with a colour that did not belong to any torch or candle. At the same time a gentle warmth spread out from it, like standing close to a banked fire on a cold night. The veins in the stone pulsed once, slowly, as though something deep inside had drawn breath.

![Tobbs and the companions lean over a booth table as an awakened Ethium Stone glows on the table, illuminating their faces in the crowded Blue Moon Alehouse](/illustrations/scenes/ch02-ethium-stone-booth.png)

*"Aethra Lumis" — and for a moment the old magic remembered itself.*

Dave made a small sound of wonder. Nibbles' eyes widened. Derek leaned closer, studying the light without touching it.

"It gives light," Tobbs said. "And warmth. That is the simplest use — the one even an apprentice can manage without setting his sleeves on fire. The stones can do more than that, in the right places and in the right hands. At shrines. At pools. Bound to old Ethium work. But first..." He looked up at them, and the cheerful mask slipped entirely. "First we need to know what is still out there."

He let the light fade by lifting his fingers from the stone. The blue glow dwindled and died, though a faint warmth lingered in the air for a moment longer.

Tobbs tapped the map where the blue marks lay scattered across the plateau. "These are places where stones may still lie hidden — or where they have begun to stir on their own." His finger moved to a different symbol, darker ink, on the western edge of the plateau where the land rose in broken ridges. "And here — the ruined tower. It's the best place to start. Old Ethium stonework, still standing. Nimozaran's notes say there was magic there once, and if the stones are waking anywhere on the plateau, a tower built to channel them is as likely a spot as any."

He traced another line, blue ink climbing beside a river mark. "Follow the river road east, then take the old track up into the hills. There's a waterfall where the only path onto the plateau climbs behind the falls — treacherous, but it avoids the open slopes where you'd be seen for miles. My master marked it for a reason." He looked up at them. "Nimozaran needs stones brought back for study before someone less careful gets there first. I know it is a great deal to ask. The road is long, the ruins are dangerous, and whoever sent goblins after Mara will not stop because we won one fight in a tavern." He swallowed. "But will you go to Ethium? Will you search the places on this map and bring back what Ethium Stones you can find?"

The table fell quiet.

Thorn looked at the map, then at Mara, then at the dull stone sitting innocently among the crumbs. "We've only just arrived," he said.

"I know."

"And people are already trying to kill us for that piece of parchment."

"Yes."

Thorn rubbed the back of his neck. It was not a refusal. Not quite. "We'll need supplies. And a proper plan."

Beside him, Dave was already nodding. "I want to see the stones. I want to learn how they work."

"Of course you do," Nibbles said. "I want to know what Nimozaran pays."

Tobbs managed a tired smile. "My master is not stingy with those who help him. And neither am I."

Derek folded his arms. "If the stones are waking, someone else will be hunting them too. Better we go with our eyes open than leave it to the Hammers."

"Or worse," Mara said quietly.

Tobbs looked at each of them in turn, hope and worry fighting in his face. "Then you'll consider it?"

"We'll consider it," Thorn said. "After we've—"

Derek leaned forward. "What other kind of things have been happening, exactly? You mentioned lights and dreams. What about little stone creatures on roofs?"

Tobbs blinked. "What?"

"A tiny stone thing," Derek said. "On the roof. With wings on its back. It flew away when it saw me."

All colour left Tobbs' face. Mara noticed immediately. "Tobbs?"

He stood so quickly his chair nearly toppled. "That was no statue. That was a homunculus — a wizard's servant. If it was watching the tavern, then someone knows the map is here."

For one horrible moment nobody moved.

Then the front doors of the Blue Moon Alehouse slammed open with such force that they bounced off the walls. The music stopped. Conversation died instantly. Four rough-looking men strode inside wearing patched leather armour, heavy boots and hard expressions. Around each of their necks hung a small iron hammer on a leather cord.

The largest of them scanned the room until his eyes settled on the map spread across the table. "There it is," he growled.

Tobbs quietly began rolling up the parchment. "I'm afraid this table is already full," he said, trying to sound cheerful.

The thug ignored him. "We're taking the map." He took another step forward.

Thorn stood. "So are we."

The gang leader smirked. "I wasn't asking." He reached for the parchment. Thorn caught his wrist, and for one heartbeat the entire tavern fell silent. Then the thug swung his free fist. Thorn ducked. The punch smashed through the edge of the table, sending bowls, bread and Dave's untouched apple pie flying across the room.

Dave gasped. "My pie!"

That was enough.

The Blue Moon exploded into chaos. The gang leader charged Thorn with a roar, swinging a heavy club that Thorn blocked with the haft of his axe before driving the man backwards into another table. Chairs toppled. Tankards flew. A sailor calmly picked up his drink before diving for cover.

Across the room another Hammer lunged towards Tobbs. Derek stepped directly into his path, and the thug's hammer crashed against his shield with a deafening clang. Derek barely moved. "You'll have to hit harder than that," he said. The thug growled and swung again. Derek smiled. "I've been head-butted by goats."

Dave had backed himself into a corner when one of the gang spotted him and decided he looked like an easy target. Dave swallowed, pointed one finger, and a potato shot across the room. It struck the thug squarely on the bridge of the nose. The man stopped running, his eyes crossed, and he slowly sat down on the floor before toppling sideways into a pile of empty barrels. The entire tavern stared.

Dave blinked. "...I meant to do that."

"You absolutely did!" shouted Thorn.

Loki barked proudly. Nibbles had vanished — then reappeared behind another Hammer with a cheerful "I'm behind you," jabbed the man sharply in the backside and swept his legs away. The thug crashed backwards over a chair that shattered with a sound the innkeeper would remember for some time.

Loki spotted another gang member trying to sneak around the side of the room towards Tobbs and launched himself through the air with tremendous enthusiasm. Dog and thug collided. The thug fell flat on his back. The map flew safely into the air. Tobbs caught it. Loki stood triumphantly on the man's chest.

"Who's a good dog?" shouted Dave, and half the tavern answered, "Loki!"

The customers had seen enough. The Hammers had started the fight, which meant everyone else was free to finish it. A fisherman grabbed a stool. A merchant threw a bread roll with surprising accuracy. Someone hit a thug over the head with a frying pan. An elderly woman poked another with her walking stick and shouted that he should get out of her tavern, even though she did not work there.

The leader of the Hammers realised the fight was slipping away. "Forget everyone else!" he pointed straight at Tobbs. "Get the map!"

For the first time Thorn saw genuine fear in the halfling's face — not fear for himself, but fear that the map might be lost. The gang leader charged. Thorn met him halfway. The two crashed together so hard they slid across the ale-soaked floor. The thug swung wildly. Thorn ducked beneath the blow and drove his shoulder into the man's chest, sending him stumbling backwards straight into the fireplace. His coat instantly caught fire. He yelped. The serving girl calmly picked up a bucket and emptied it over his head. Steam filled the room.

Nibbles couldn't resist. "Looking a bit warm?" Nobody laughed. It wasn't his strongest joke.

Another Hammer made a desperate dash for the door. Dave panicked and fired another potato, which missed completely and sailed through the kitchen doorway. A loud crash echoed from somewhere beyond, followed by someone shouting, "My soup!"

"I'm really sorry!" Dave called.

"You can apologise later!" Thorn shouted back.

The remaining gang members looked around the tavern. Their leader was soaked. One man was trapped beneath Loki. Another had been knocked unconscious by a potato. The customers were advancing armed with stools, mugs and whatever else they could find. One Hammer quietly lowered his club. "I think we should leave." Nobody argued.

The gang fled — one slipping on mashed potato, another running into the doorframe before finding the doorway properly — and within seconds they had vanished into the streets of Lowtown.

Silence settled over the Blue Moon. Then somebody began clapping, and soon the whole tavern erupted into applause. The fisherman raised his mug. "To the youngsters!" someone shouted. "To Loki!" cried another. "To the potato wizard!" Dave looked delighted.

The innkeeper surveyed the damage — three broken tables, five broken chairs, ale everywhere, potato everywhere — and folded her arms. "It could have been worse."

"How?" asked Nibbles.

"The roof's still on."

That seemed reasonable.

Only a few minutes later Sergeant Murgaddin arrived with half a dozen guards. He stopped in the doorway, looked around the wrecked tavern, then looked back at the companions. "I was only gone for an hour."

"It wasn't entirely our fault," Derek said.

Murgaddin sighed. "I know." He crouched beside one of the unconscious Hammers and lifted the small iron pendant from around the man's neck. "The Hammers. They've become bolder."

Tobbs carefully tucked the map back into Mara's satchel. "They knew exactly what they were looking for."

Murgaddin nodded grimly. "They're not working alone. The Hammers are one part of something much larger — one of the criminal guilds that have united in Sunfall, the capital. The Gang of Four. Their reach stretches further north every year, and I've been trying to find their hideout near Fallcrest for months." He looked directly at Thorn. "I think you might be exactly the people I need."

Tobbs waited until the guards had carried the last groaning Hammer out into the square before he spoke again. The Ethium Stone was back in his satchel, wrapped in cloth. The map was safe with Mara. But his eyes kept returning to the companions.

"You still mean to consider it?" he asked Thorn quietly. "Ethium won't wait for us to finish arguing."

Thorn looked at his friends — at Dave's eager face, at Nibbles counting imaginary coin, at Derek's steady nod, at Loki, who had fallen asleep with one paw on a sausage.

"We'll talk properly in the morning," Thorn said. "But yes. I think we're going."

Outside, the bells of Fallcrest rang the hour.

Far beyond the farms, forests and open plains, the forgotten ruins of Ethium waited in silence.
